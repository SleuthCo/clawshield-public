# Channel Strategy for Communications & Outreach

## The PESO Model: Paid, Earned, Shared, Owned

The PESO model, developed by Gini Dietrich, is the foundational framework for modern communications channel planning. It recognizes that communications channels operate across four distinct but interconnected categories, and effective strategy requires orchestration across all four.

### Paid Media

Paid media encompasses any channel where the organization pays for placement or distribution. In a communications context (distinct from performance marketing), paid media includes sponsored content and native advertising in industry publications, social media advertising for message amplification, paid search for reputation management and narrative control, sponsored events, webinars, and conference sessions, influencer partnerships with compensation, and paid media distribution services such as press release wire services.

The communications value of paid media lies in guaranteed reach, controlled messaging, and audience targeting precision. The limitation is credibility — audiences increasingly discount paid placements and may distrust overtly promotional content. Paid media is most effective when it amplifies earned or owned content rather than serving as the primary narrative vehicle.

### Earned Media

Earned media is coverage, mentions, and placement that the organization receives without direct payment. This includes news articles, broadcast segments, and podcast features driven by media relations, analyst reports and industry coverage, organic social media mentions and shares, word-of-mouth recommendations, speaking invitations and panel selections, and awards and recognition from third parties.

Earned media carries the highest credibility because it is implicitly endorsed by the third party publishing or sharing it. The trade-off is lack of control — journalists write their own narratives, analysts form independent opinions, and social conversations are unpredictable. Effective earned media strategy requires strong relationships, newsworthy content, and rapid response capability.

### Shared Media

Shared media refers to content distributed through social platforms where both the organization and the audience participate in content creation and distribution. This includes company social media profiles and the content published through them, social media engagement (comments, replies, community management), employee advocacy programs where staff share organizational content, user-generated content campaigns, online community forums and discussion platforms, and collaborative content such as Twitter/X Spaces, LinkedIn Lives, and Reddit AMAs.

Shared media blurs the line between broadcast and conversation. It offers reach and engagement but subjects the organization to public dialogue, including criticism. The strategic value of shared media is its ability to humanize the organization and create two-way relationships with stakeholders.

### Owned Media

Owned media comprises channels fully controlled by the organization. These include the corporate website and blog, email newsletters and marketing automation sequences, podcasts and video channels (YouTube, Vimeo), mobile applications, whitepapers, e-books, and research reports, intranet and employee communication platforms, and annual reports and corporate publications.

Owned media offers complete control over content, timing, and presentation. It serves as the hub to which all other channel activities should drive traffic and engagement. The limitation is that owned media requires sustained investment in content quality and audience building — it has no inherent distribution mechanism.

## Channel Selection Criteria

### The REACH Framework for Channel Evaluation

When selecting channels for a communications initiative, evaluate each option against five criteria:

**Relevance:** Does the target audience actively use this channel? A LinkedIn thought leadership campaign is highly relevant for B2B executive audiences but irrelevant for consumer segments. Channel relevance should be validated through audience research, not assumed.

**Effectiveness:** Does the channel deliver the desired communications outcome? A press release on a wire service creates broad awareness but limited engagement. A targeted briefing with an industry analyst generates deep credibility with a narrow audience. Match channel effectiveness to the communications objective.

**Accessibility:** Can the organization practically execute on this channel? Effective podcast production requires audio equipment, editing capability, consistent scheduling, and host talent. If these resources are unavailable, the channel is inaccessible regardless of its theoretical value.

**Cost:** What is the total cost of ownership, including content creation, distribution, management, monitoring, and measurement? Evaluate cost per reach, cost per engagement, and cost per outcome to enable cross-channel comparison.

**Harmony:** Does the channel complement the broader channel mix? Adding a channel that fragments the audience or creates message inconsistency reduces the overall effectiveness of the channel strategy. Each channel should reinforce rather than compete with others.

### Channel-Audience Fit Matrix

Map channels against audience segments to identify coverage and gaps:

**C-Suite Executives:** Respond best to exclusive, concise formats. Preferred channels include one-on-one briefings, executive roundtables, curated email newsletters (not mass distribution), tier-1 media coverage, and analyst reports. Avoid high-volume, low-signal channels.

**Technical Audiences (Engineers, Developers):** Value depth, accuracy, and peer credibility. Preferred channels include technical blogs, developer documentation, conference talks (not marketing-themed), GitHub and Stack Overflow presence, and podcasts with technical hosts. Reject overt marketing framing.

**Investors and Analysts:** Require structured, verifiable information. Preferred channels include earnings calls and IR webcasts, SEC filings and investor decks, analyst day presentations, financial media (Bloomberg, WSJ, FT), and direct analyst engagement. Regulatory compliance governs channel selection.

**Employees:** Expect transparency, timeliness, and relevance. Preferred channels include all-hands meetings, intranet and collaboration platforms (Slack, Teams), manager cascade communications, employee newsletters, and town hall Q&A sessions. Employees should receive major news before external audiences.

**Media:** Require exclusivity, timeliness, and access. Preferred channels include embargoed briefings, press releases (for broad announcements), one-on-one interviews, press conferences (for major events), and responsive spokesperson availability. Relationships and trust are the primary currency.

## Multi-Channel Orchestration

### The Hub-and-Spoke Model

Designate the corporate website or a primary owned platform as the hub — the authoritative source of organizational content. All other channels (spokes) drive traffic and attention to the hub while adapting content for their specific audience and format requirements.

For example, a major product announcement might orchestrate as follows: The hub publishes a comprehensive blog post with full details, visuals, and customer quotes. The press release (earned spoke) provides a news-format summary with spokesperson quotes and links to the hub. Social media posts (shared spokes) offer bite-sized highlights with visual assets and links to the hub. An email newsletter (owned spoke) delivers a personalized summary to subscribers with a call to action to visit the hub. Paid social promotion (paid spoke) amplifies the highest-performing organic social content.

### Sequencing and Timing

Multi-channel orchestration requires deliberate sequencing. A general framework for a major announcement:

**T-minus 7 days:** Prepare all channel assets. Brief internal teams. Secure media embargoes. Schedule social content.

**T-minus 1 day:** Final legal and executive review. Send embargoed materials to media. Brief customer success and sales teams. Prepare internal communications.

**T-zero (announcement time):** Publish hub content. Distribute press release. Send internal communication. Post initial social media content. Activate employee advocacy. Begin paid amplification.

**T-plus 1 hour:** Monitor media pickup and social conversation. Respond to journalist inquiries. Address employee questions. Adjust social content based on initial response.

**T-plus 24 hours:** Publish follow-up content (FAQ, deeper dive, customer reaction). Begin analyst outreach. Assess first-day metrics. Adjust paid spend based on performance.

**T-plus 1 week:** Compile initial results. Share coverage digest with stakeholders. Plan sustaining content. Identify opportunities for follow-up stories.

### Content Adaptation Principles

Content must be adapted — not merely copied — for each channel. Adaptation principles include format matching (long-form for blog, short-form for social, structured for press release), tone matching (conversational for social, formal for investor communications, technical for developer content), visual matching (infographic for social, data tables for analyst briefings, screenshots for technical blog), and call-to-action matching (share for social, read more for email, contact for sales enablement).

## Digital vs. Traditional Channel Considerations

### Digital Channel Advantages

Digital channels offer precise targeting through demographic, behavioral, and intent data. They provide real-time performance data enabling rapid optimization. Digital channels support interactive and two-way engagement. They offer lower distribution costs per reach compared to traditional channels. Content has extended shelf life and discoverability through search. Attribution and ROI measurement are more feasible.

### Traditional Channel Advantages

Traditional channels (print media, broadcast television, radio, events, direct mail) retain advantages in certain contexts. They provide credibility signals that digital channels lack — a Wall Street Journal feature carries implicit validation. Physical presence at events creates relationship depth that digital cannot replicate. Traditional media reaches audiences that are less digitally engaged, including certain executive and government audiences. Print materials in high-quality formats signal premium positioning.

### Hybrid Approaches

The most effective channel strategies integrate digital and traditional. A physical event is live-streamed and clipped for social content. A print feature is amplified through digital channels and repurposed as a social proof asset. A direct mail piece includes a QR code linking to a digital experience. Hybrid approaches maximize the strengths of each channel type while mitigating their limitations.

## Measurement Framework for Channel Strategy

### Channel-Level Metrics

**Reach Metrics:** Impressions, circulation, unique visitors, follower count, email list size, event attendance. These quantify the potential audience exposed to the message.

**Engagement Metrics:** Click-through rates, time on page, social interactions (likes, comments, shares), email open and click rates, event participation rates, media inquiry volume. These measure audience response and interest.

**Conversion Metrics:** Lead generation, website goal completions, content downloads, meeting requests, spokesperson interview requests, event registration. These connect communications activity to business-relevant actions.

**Sentiment Metrics:** Net sentiment score, share of positive/negative/neutral coverage, comment sentiment analysis, Net Promoter Score. These assess qualitative audience response.

### Cross-Channel Attribution

Attributing outcomes to specific channels is the most challenging aspect of communications measurement. Approaches include multi-touch attribution models that assign weighted credit to each channel touchpoint, marketing mix modeling that uses statistical analysis to estimate each channel's contribution to outcomes, controlled experiments where channels are activated and deactivated in specific markets to measure incremental impact, and survey-based attribution where audiences self-report how they became aware of or influenced by the organization.

No attribution model is perfect. Adopt a consistent methodology, acknowledge its limitations, and use directional insights to optimize channel investment rather than treating attribution outputs as precise measurements.

### Channel Portfolio Optimization

Review channel portfolio performance quarterly. For each channel, calculate cost per outcome and compare across the portfolio. Identify channels that are underperforming relative to investment and either optimize or reallocate resources. Identify channels that are overperforming and explore opportunities to increase investment. Monitor emerging channels for potential addition to the portfolio. Retire channels that no longer serve strategic objectives or reach target audiences effectively.

The channel strategy should evolve continuously as audience behaviors shift, new platforms emerge, and organizational priorities change. Annual strategic reviews should reassess the fundamental channel architecture, while quarterly operational reviews optimize execution within the existing architecture.
