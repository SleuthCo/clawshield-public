# Communication Frameworks & Principles

## The PESO Model

The PESO model organizes media and content strategies into four interconnected channels.

**Paid Media**: Sponsored content, advertising, paid social, PPC campaigns, influencer partnerships. You control the message and placement but must earn attention despite the "paid" label. Best for: reach amplification, targeted audience acquisition, product launches.

**Earned Media**: Press coverage, analyst mentions, organic reviews, word-of-mouth, social shares. Highest credibility but lowest control. Requires compelling narratives and genuine newsworthiness. Best for: brand credibility, thought leadership validation, crisis response.

**Shared Media**: Social media engagement, community building, co-created content, partnerships. Semi-controlled — you initiate but the audience shapes the conversation. Best for: community engagement, feedback loops, brand personality.

**Owned Media**: Blog, website, newsletters, documentation, podcasts, white papers. Full control over content and distribution. Best for: SEO, thought leadership, nurture sequences, detailed technical content.

**Integration principle**: The most effective campaigns activate all four channels with consistent messaging adapted to each channel's norms and audience expectations.

## The Message House Framework

A Message House structures all communications into a hierarchical architecture.

**Roof (Core Message)**: One sentence that encapsulates your entire position. Everything below supports this. It should be memorable, defensible, and differentiated. Example: "Our platform makes enterprise AI safe by design, not by afterthought."

**Pillars (Supporting Messages)**: Three to four key themes that support the core message. Each pillar should be independently compelling and collectively exhaustive. Example pillars: (1) Security is embedded at the architecture level, (2) Teams ship faster with built-in guardrails, (3) Compliance is continuous, not a checkbox.

**Foundation (Proof Points)**: Specific evidence supporting each pillar — statistics, case studies, customer quotes, third-party validations, competitive benchmarks. Each pillar needs at least three proof points.

**Application**: Every piece of content (email, blog, presentation, tweet) should trace back to one or more pillars. If it doesn't support the house, question whether it should exist.

## Stakeholder Communication Matrix

Effective communication requires mapping stakeholders and tailoring approach by influence and interest.

**High Influence / High Interest (Manage Closely)**: These are your key decision-makers. Provide detailed, frequent updates. Engage in two-way dialogue. Anticipate their concerns proactively. Examples: executive sponsors, board members, key customers.

**High Influence / Low Interest (Keep Satisfied)**: These stakeholders can block or enable your work but aren't deeply engaged day-to-day. Communicate efficiently — executive summaries, dashboards, milestone updates. Don't overwhelm with detail. Examples: C-suite peers, regulatory bodies.

**Low Influence / High Interest (Keep Informed)**: These people care deeply but can't directly influence outcomes. Regular updates and inclusion in feedback loops builds advocacy. Examples: individual contributors, community members, enthusiast users.

**Low Influence / Low Interest (Monitor)**: Minimal communication required. Periodic broad updates via owned channels. Examples: general public, tangential teams.

## The SCR Framework (Situation-Complication-Resolution)

The most effective structure for persuasive business communication.

**Situation**: Establish the shared context everyone agrees on. This grounds the audience and builds rapport. Keep it brief — one to two sentences. "Our team ships 40 releases per month to production."

**Complication**: Introduce the tension, problem, or change that demands attention. This creates urgency. "However, 23% of those releases contain at least one critical vulnerability that isn't caught until post-deployment."

**Resolution**: Present your recommendation or path forward. This is where you earn the audience's engagement. "By integrating security scanning into the CI/CD pipeline at the PR stage, we can catch 90% of these issues before they reach production — with zero impact to release velocity."

**Why it works**: SCR mirrors how the brain processes narrative. It creates a knowledge gap (complication) that the audience wants filled (resolution). It's effective in emails, presentations, proposals, and one-pagers.

## The Pyramid Principle (Minto)

Barbara Minto's Pyramid Principle: always lead with the answer, then support with grouped arguments.

**BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)**: State your recommendation or conclusion first. Busy stakeholders may only read the first paragraph. If they do, they should have your key message.

**Grouping**: Support your answer with three to five logically grouped arguments. Each group should be MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive).

**Ordering**: Within each group, order by: importance (most critical first), chronology (when sequence matters), or structure (when building a framework).

**Anti-pattern**: Starting with background, then analysis, then finally the recommendation buried at the end. This forces the reader to hold all context in memory before understanding why it matters.

## Tone Calibration by Audience

**Engineering teams**: Lead with technical specifics. Be direct. Use concrete examples and data. Avoid corporate jargon. Acknowledge complexity honestly.

**Executive leadership**: Lead with business impact and risk framing. Use the SCR or Pyramid structure. Quantify everything possible. Keep it to one page unless asked for detail.

**Customers and prospects**: Lead with their problem, not your solution. Use their language, not yours. Show, don't tell — case studies, demos, proof points. Be honest about limitations.

**Board of Directors**: Maximum compression. One key message per topic. Risk/opportunity framing. Forward-looking. No surprises — if there's bad news, lead with it and follow with the mitigation plan.

**Cross-functional peers**: Balance detail with context. Acknowledge dependencies explicitly. Frame requests as shared goals. Use collaborative language ("we" over "you should").

## Crisis Communication Principles

**The Golden Hour**: The first 60 minutes after an incident becomes public define the narrative. If you don't fill the information vacuum, others will — with speculation.

**Acknowledge, Empathize, Act**: (1) Acknowledge what happened factually. (2) Express genuine empathy for those affected. (3) Describe concrete actions being taken.

**What to say when you don't know**: "We are actively investigating. Here is what we know so far. We will provide updates every [timeframe]. Our priority is [affected parties]."

**What never to say**: "No comment." Blame-shifting language. Minimizing language ("minor incident"). Premature root cause attribution. Legal hedging that sounds evasive.

**Holding statement structure**: Facts confirmed so far. Actions taken. Next update timing. Where to find more information. Who to contact.

## Writing for Technical Audiences

**The inverted pyramid**: Most important information first. Supporting detail in descending order of importance. Readers should be able to stop reading at any paragraph and still have the key information.

**Active voice**: "The team discovered the vulnerability" not "The vulnerability was discovered by the team." Active voice is clearer, more direct, and takes fewer words.

**One idea per paragraph**: Each paragraph should advance exactly one point. If you're covering two ideas, split into two paragraphs.

**Jargon rules**: Use technical terms when writing for technical audiences — precision matters more than accessibility. Define terms when writing for mixed audiences. Never use jargon to sound impressive.

**Length discipline**: If it can be said in 50 words, don't use 200. Every sentence should earn its place.
