# Brand Voice Guidelines for Communications & Outreach

## Defining Brand Voice vs. Tone

Brand voice is the consistent personality expressed through all organizational communications. It remains constant regardless of audience, channel, or context. Voice reflects who the organization is — its values, character, and identity.

Tone is the emotional inflection applied to the voice in a specific situation. While the voice stays the same, the tone adapts. A brand with an "approachable and knowledgeable" voice may adopt a reassuring tone during a crisis, a celebratory tone during a product launch, and a serious tone in regulatory communications. The voice is the constant; the tone is the variable.

This distinction is critical for communications teams managing multiple channels, audiences, and content types. Writers must internalize the voice and then apply appropriate tone judgment for each context.

## Voice Architecture Framework

### The Four Dimensions of Voice

**Formality Spectrum:** Where does the organization sit between formal and casual? A financial services firm typically occupies the formal end; a consumer tech brand may be decidedly casual. Most B2B organizations sit in the "professional but approachable" middle zone. Define the acceptable range — even casual brands have moments requiring formality (legal notices, crisis communications).

**Personality Spectrum:** What human characteristics does the brand embody? Common brand personality dimensions include sincerity (honest, wholesome, cheerful), excitement (daring, spirited, imaginative), competence (reliable, intelligent, successful), sophistication (upper class, charming, refined), and ruggedness (outdoorsy, tough, sturdy). Select 2-3 primary personality traits and define how they manifest in written and verbal communications.

**Emotion Spectrum:** What emotional register does the brand operate in? Some brands lead with inspiration and optimism. Others prioritize calm authority. Some emphasize empathy and warmth. The emotional spectrum guides word choice, sentence structure, and narrative framing.

**Complexity Spectrum:** How does the brand approach complexity in communications? Some brands simplify everything for maximum accessibility. Others embrace technical depth as a credibility signal. Most communications functions must operate across this spectrum, simplifying for executive summaries and general audiences while maintaining depth for technical and specialist audiences.

### Voice Documentation Structure

A comprehensive brand voice guide should include a voice summary statement (1-2 sentences capturing the overall voice), personality traits with definitions and examples (what the trait means in practice, not just an adjective), "this not that" tables showing the distinction between on-brand and off-brand expressions, word choice guidelines including preferred vocabulary and forbidden terms, example passages demonstrating the voice across multiple content types, and tone adaptation guidelines for different contexts (celebratory, empathetic, authoritative, educational, urgent).

## Writing Style Guide Essentials

### Clarity and Readability

**Sentence Length:** Average sentence length should be 15-20 words for general audiences. Technical content may extend to 25 words per sentence. Vary sentence length for rhythm — monotonous sentence length creates fatigue.

**Paragraph Length:** Limit paragraphs to 3-5 sentences for digital content. Long paragraphs create visual density that reduces readability, particularly on mobile devices. One-sentence paragraphs are acceptable for emphasis.

**Active Voice:** Prefer active voice in all communications. "The team launched the product" is stronger than "The product was launched by the team." Passive voice is appropriate when the actor is unknown, irrelevant, or strategically de-emphasized (common in crisis communications: "The issue has been identified and is being resolved").

**Jargon Management:** Eliminate unnecessary jargon. When technical terms are required, define them on first use or provide contextual explanation. Create a glossary for recurring technical terms. Avoid acronyms unless they are more recognizable than the spelled-out version (FBI, CEO, API are acceptable; BFCM, TAM, or OKR may not be for all audiences).

**Reading Level:** Target 8th-grade reading level (Flesch-Kincaid) for general communications. Executive communications may target 10th-12th grade. Technical documentation targets the appropriate reading level for the skilled audience. Use readability tools to verify, but do not sacrifice accuracy for simplicity.

### Grammar and Mechanics

**Oxford Comma:** Adopt a consistent position and enforce it. The Oxford comma (serial comma) reduces ambiguity in lists and is recommended for most professional communications.

**Capitalization:** Define a capitalization policy for product names, feature names, department names, and titles. Inconsistent capitalization is one of the most common brand consistency failures. Title case for headings, sentence case for subheadings is a common standard.

**Numbers:** Spell out numbers one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above. Exceptions: always use numerals for percentages (5%), currency ($7), dates, and technical specifications. Be consistent in lists — if any number in a list is 10 or above, use numerals for all items.

**Dates and Times:** Define the standard format. ISO 8601 (2025-06-01) is unambiguous for international audiences. "June 1, 2025" is common in US communications. Include time zones for all time references in global communications.

**Pronouns:** Use "we" to refer to the organization in most external communications, reinforcing the organizational voice. Use "you" to address the audience directly, creating engagement. Avoid "one" as an impersonal pronoun — it reads as overly formal. Refer to individuals using their stated pronouns.

### Formatting Standards

**Headlines:** Write headlines in active voice with strong verbs. Headlines should convey the key message independently — many readers scan headlines without reading body copy. Avoid clickbait formulations that sacrifice credibility for engagement.

**Subheadings:** Use subheadings every 2-4 paragraphs in long-form content to enable scanning. Subheadings should be informative, not clever — they serve as navigation aids.

**Lists:** Use bulleted lists for unordered items and numbered lists for sequential steps or ranked items. Keep list items parallel in grammatical structure. If one item starts with a verb, all items should start with verbs.

**Bold and Italics:** Use bold for emphasis of key terms or phrases. Use italics for titles of publications, foreign words, or when introducing a defined term. Do not use bold for entire paragraphs — it defeats the purpose of emphasis.

## Brand Consistency Across Channels

### Consistency Framework

Brand consistency requires alignment across five dimensions: visual consistency (logos, colors, typography, imagery), verbal consistency (voice, tone, messaging, vocabulary), behavioral consistency (customer service interactions, sales conversations, executive appearances), temporal consistency (maintaining coherent narrative across time, even as strategy evolves), and cross-channel consistency (ensuring the same brand experience regardless of touchpoint).

### Common Consistency Failures

**Spokesperson Drift:** Individual spokespeople develop personal styles that diverge from brand voice. Regular media training and message discipline reviews are necessary to maintain alignment.

**Channel-Specific Silos:** Social media, PR, internal communications, and marketing may develop distinct voices when teams operate independently. Cross-functional editorial reviews and shared style guides prevent divergence.

**Agency Inconsistency:** External agencies and contractors may not fully internalize the brand voice. Comprehensive onboarding, detailed briefs, and consistent editorial oversight are required.

**Localization Gaps:** Translated content may lose voice characteristics if translators prioritize literal accuracy over brand personality. Use transcreation (creative translation) rather than direct translation for brand-critical content.

**Temporal Drift:** Over time, the brand voice may evolve informally without deliberate updates to the style guide. Annual voice audits ensure documentation matches actual practice.

### Brand Voice Governance

Establish a brand voice governance structure that includes a style guide owner (typically the Head of Brand or Communications), an editorial review process for new content types and channels, a quarterly voice consistency audit across channels, a training program for new team members and external partners, and an exception process for situations requiring deliberate voice deviation (crisis, legal, regulatory).

## Inclusive Language Guidelines

### Principles of Inclusive Communication

Inclusive language acknowledges and respects the diversity of the audience. It avoids assumptions about gender, race, ethnicity, age, ability, sexual orientation, socioeconomic status, or any other personal characteristic. Inclusive language is not merely avoiding offense — it is actively ensuring that all members of the audience feel recognized and valued.

### Gender-Inclusive Language

Use gender-neutral terms where possible. Replace "chairman" with "chair" or "chairperson." Replace "manpower" with "workforce" or "staffing." Replace "man-made" with "artificial" or "synthetic." Use "they/them" as singular pronouns when gender is unknown or when individuals use those pronouns. Avoid gendered metaphors and idioms ("man up," "rule of thumb" in some contexts).

When referring to specific individuals, use their stated name and pronouns. When writing about hypothetical individuals, vary examples to include diverse names and avoid defaulting to a single gender.

### Ability-Inclusive Language

Use person-first language when referring to disability unless the individual or community prefers identity-first language. "Person with a disability" rather than "disabled person." "Person who uses a wheelchair" rather than "wheelchair-bound." Avoid language that frames disability as limitation or suffering — "suffers from" should be replaced with "has" or "lives with."

Avoid ableist metaphors in business communications: "blind spot" can become "gap" or "oversight." "Tone-deaf" can become "insensitive" or "out of touch." "Crippling" can become "severe" or "damaging." These substitutions improve precision while removing exclusionary language.

### Cultural and Ethnic Sensitivity

Capitalize racial and ethnic identifiers (Black, Indigenous, Latino/a/e). Avoid monolithic characterizations of cultural groups. Use specific terms over general ones when possible (Mexican-American rather than Hispanic, if applicable). Avoid idioms with racist origins or connotations. When communicating globally, account for cultural norms around directness, formality, humor, and imagery.

### Socioeconomic Sensitivity

Avoid assumptions about audience members' economic circumstances. Use "affordable" rather than "cheap." Avoid framing premium products as inherently superior — emphasize value rather than exclusivity. In employee communications, be sensitive to the range of economic experiences within the workforce.

## Accessibility in Communications

### Digital Accessibility Standards

All digital communications should comply with WCAG 2.1 Level AA guidelines at minimum. Key requirements for communications content include providing alt text for all images, including decorative images (which should have empty alt attributes), ensuring sufficient color contrast (4.5:1 ratio for normal text, 3:1 for large text), not relying on color alone to convey information, using proper heading hierarchy (H1, H2, H3) for document structure, providing captions for video content and transcripts for audio content, ensuring all interactive elements are keyboard-accessible, and using descriptive link text (not "click here").

### Plain Language

Write in plain language as the default. Plain language means the audience can find what they need, understand what they find, and use what they find to meet their needs — all on the first reading. Techniques include using common words over specialized terms, breaking complex ideas into digestible components, leading with the most important information, using examples to illustrate abstract concepts, and testing content with representative audience members.

### Multi-Format Delivery

Provide important communications in multiple formats to maximize accessibility. A video announcement should also be available as a written summary. A detailed report should include an executive summary. A podcast episode should have a written transcript. An infographic should have an accessible data table alternative.

### Cognitive Accessibility

Structure content to reduce cognitive load. Use consistent navigation and layout patterns. Provide clear signposting for content structure. Avoid ambiguity in instructions or calls to action. Allow sufficient time for engagement with time-limited content. Offer ways to revisit or review information.

## Voice Application Across Content Types

### Press Releases

Maintain formal-to-professional tone. Follow AP or relevant style guide for media. Lead with the most newsworthy information. Use direct, declarative statements. Include executive quotes that sound natural and convey perspective rather than marketing language. Close with standard boilerplate that reflects current brand positioning.

### Blog Posts

Adopt a more conversational tone within the brand voice range. Use first person plural ("we") to create connection. Address the reader directly ("you"). Break content into scannable sections. Include practical value — insights, how-to guidance, or informed perspective. Avoid pure promotional content.

### Social Media

Adapt the voice for platform conventions while maintaining brand personality. Brevity, visual engagement, and conversational tone are priorities. Use platform-specific features (hashtags, mentions, polls) strategically. Respond to engagement promptly and in voice.

### Executive Communications

Apply the most authoritative register of the brand voice. Ensure messages reflect the executive's personal style while remaining on-brand. Use data and evidence to support positions. Maintain appropriate formality for the context (keynote vs. blog post vs. internal email).

### Crisis Communications

Prioritize clarity, empathy, and accountability. Strip away marketing language entirely. Use simple, direct sentences. Acknowledge the situation, express concern for affected parties, describe actions being taken, and commit to ongoing communication. Avoid legalistic or evasive language that undermines trust.
