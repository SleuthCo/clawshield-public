# Stakeholder Analysis for Communications & Outreach

## Overview of Stakeholder Analysis in Communications

Stakeholder analysis is the systematic process of identifying individuals, groups, and organizations that can affect or be affected by an organization's communications activities. For the Communications & Outreach function, stakeholder analysis serves as the foundational discipline that determines message targeting, channel selection, resource allocation, and engagement sequencing. Effective stakeholder analysis reduces wasted effort, prevents message misalignment, and ensures that limited communications resources are directed toward the audiences with the greatest impact on organizational objectives.

The communications-specific stakeholder analysis differs from general project management stakeholder analysis in several key ways: it must account for message amplification potential, media influence chains, narrative shaping power, and the interconnected nature of public discourse. A stakeholder who is low-power in a traditional business context may wield outsized influence through social media reach or journalistic access.

## Stakeholder Mapping Frameworks

### The Power-Interest Grid (Mendelow's Matrix)

The Power-Interest Grid classifies stakeholders along two axes: their power to influence the organization and their level of interest in organizational activities.

**High Power / High Interest (Key Players):** These stakeholders require the most active engagement. In a communications context, this includes the CEO and C-suite, board members, major investors, key journalists covering the company, regulatory bodies with oversight authority, and strategic partners. Communications strategy should prioritize these stakeholders with personalized, proactive, and frequent engagement. They should receive advance notice of announcements, have direct access to spokespeople, and be included in feedback loops.

**High Power / Low Interest (Keep Satisfied):** These stakeholders can significantly impact the organization but are not regularly engaged. Examples include government officials, industry association leaders, dormant institutional investors, and enterprise customers with large contracts. Communications should provide periodic, concise updates that respect their time while keeping the organization on their radar. Quarterly briefings, executive summaries, and targeted outreach around relevant policy changes are appropriate tactics.

**Low Power / High Interest (Keep Informed):** These stakeholders are engaged and attentive but have limited direct influence. They include employees below senior leadership, community groups, small shareholders, industry bloggers, and academic researchers. Regular information sharing through newsletters, community forums, intranet updates, and social media engagement satisfies their information needs and can convert them into advocates.

**Low Power / Low Interest (Monitor):** These stakeholders require minimal direct communication but should be monitored for shifts in power or interest. They include the general public, distant competitors, and peripheral industry analysts. Broad communications channels such as press releases, website content, and annual reports serve this group.

### The Salience Model (Mitchell, Agle & Wood)

The Salience Model evaluates stakeholders on three attributes: power (ability to impose will), legitimacy (appropriateness of their claim), and urgency (time sensitivity of their claim). Stakeholders possessing all three attributes are "definitive stakeholders" and demand immediate communications attention. Those with two attributes are "expectant stakeholders," and those with one are "latent stakeholders."

For communications planning, this model is particularly useful during crisis situations where urgency shifts rapidly. A community group (legitimacy only) that experiences a chemical spill near their neighborhood suddenly gains urgency, becoming a "dependent stakeholder" requiring immediate outreach.

### The Stakeholder Influence Network

Beyond individual classification, communications professionals must map influence relationships between stakeholders. An industry analyst with moderate direct power may have outsized influence because they advise institutional investors (high power). A mid-level journalist may be the primary source for a Tier 1 outlet editor.

Mapping these networks involves identifying: information flow paths between stakeholders, trust relationships and credibility chains, shared affiliations and group memberships, historical communication patterns, and social media connection density.

## Influence Strategies by Stakeholder Type

### Direct Influence Strategies

**Rational Persuasion:** Presenting logical arguments and factual evidence. Most effective with analytically oriented stakeholders such as investors, regulators, and technical audiences. Communications deliverables include white papers, data-driven presentations, research reports, and detailed briefing documents.

**Inspirational Appeals:** Connecting to values, ideals, and aspirations. Effective with employees, community stakeholders, and purpose-driven partners. Communications deliverables include vision narratives, impact stories, culture manifestos, and mission-driven content.

**Consultation:** Involving stakeholders in planning or decision-making. Appropriate for key customers, strategic partners, and employee representatives. Communications deliverables include advisory council agendas, co-creation workshop materials, feedback surveys, and collaborative roadmaps.

### Indirect Influence Strategies

**Coalition Building:** Engaging allies to support a position with a target stakeholder. When a regulator is skeptical of a company's data practices, testimonials from industry peers, endorsements from privacy advocates, and supportive academic research can shift the narrative.

**Legitimating:** Aligning communications with accepted norms, precedents, or authorities. Referencing industry standards, regulatory frameworks, or recognized thought leaders adds credibility when engaging skeptical stakeholders.

**Social Proof:** Demonstrating that peers or respected entities have adopted a similar position. Case studies, customer testimonials, and industry benchmarking reports serve this strategy.

## Engagement Planning

### Stakeholder Engagement Levels

Define five levels of engagement intensity for communications planning:

**Level 1 — Inform:** One-way communication providing stakeholders with balanced and objective information. Channels include press releases, annual reports, website updates, and mass email communications. Frequency is periodic and event-driven.

**Level 2 — Consult:** Two-way communication seeking stakeholder feedback on specific topics. Channels include surveys, focus groups, public comment periods, and social media polls. Feedback is acknowledged and considered but does not necessarily drive decisions.

**Level 3 — Involve:** Ongoing two-way communication ensuring stakeholder concerns are understood and considered. Channels include advisory panels, regular meetings, community forums, and dedicated relationship managers. This level requires sustained resource commitment.

**Level 4 — Collaborate:** Joint decision-making on specific communications or organizational matters. Channels include joint working groups, co-authored publications, shared governance structures, and partnership communications. Both parties share accountability for outcomes.

**Level 5 — Empower:** Placing decision-making authority in the hands of stakeholders. This is rare in corporate communications but appears in community-governed initiatives, employee-led culture programs, and open-source community management.

### Engagement Plan Template

A comprehensive stakeholder engagement plan for communications includes the following elements for each stakeholder or stakeholder group:

- Stakeholder name/group and primary contact
- Classification (using the chosen framework)
- Current relationship quality (1-5 scale)
- Target relationship quality
- Key concerns and information needs
- Preferred communication channels and formats
- Engagement level (1-5)
- Message themes and key narratives
- Engagement frequency and cadence
- Responsible communications team member
- Success metrics and evaluation criteria
- Risk factors and contingency approaches

### Engagement Sequencing

The order in which stakeholders are engaged around a major announcement matters significantly. A general sequencing framework for a major corporate announcement:

1. Board of Directors (approval and awareness)
2. Executive Leadership Team (alignment and preparation)
3. Legal and Compliance (review and clearance)
4. Investor Relations / Major Shareholders (if material, per regulatory requirements)
5. Employees (before external audiences whenever possible)
6. Strategic Partners and Key Customers (courtesy advance notice)
7. Media (embargo or simultaneous with public release)
8. General Public (press release, social media, website)
9. Industry Analysts and Influencers (post-announcement briefings)

Disruption of this sequence creates risk. Employees learning major news from media reports before internal communication damages trust and engagement. Investors discovering material information through social media creates regulatory and legal exposure.

## RACI Matrix for Communications Activities

### Defining RACI Roles in Communications

**Responsible (R):** The person or team that performs the communications work. This is the writer, designer, or strategist who creates the deliverable.

**Accountable (A):** The single person who has final authority and approval over the communications deliverable. In communications, this is often the VP of Communications, Chief Communications Officer, or the relevant business unit leader.

**Consulted (C):** Individuals whose input is sought before or during the creation of the communication. Subject matter experts, legal counsel, and affected business unit leaders typically fill this role.

**Informed (I):** Individuals who need to know about the communication but do not provide input into its creation. Broader leadership, adjacent teams, and downstream stakeholders are typically informed.

### Sample RACI for Common Communications Activities

**Press Release:**
- Responsible: Communications Manager / PR Lead
- Accountable: VP of Communications
- Consulted: Legal, Subject Matter Expert, Investor Relations (if material)
- Informed: CEO, relevant business unit heads, social media team

**Crisis Response Statement:**
- Responsible: Crisis Communications Lead
- Accountable: Chief Communications Officer
- Consulted: Legal, CEO, CISO (if cyber), Operations (if operational)
- Informed: Board Chair, all employees (post-release), customer success team

**Employee Town Hall:**
- Responsible: Internal Communications Manager
- Accountable: CHRO or CEO (depending on topic)
- Consulted: HR Business Partners, relevant business leaders, legal (if sensitive)
- Informed: All people managers (advance briefing), full employee base

**Investor Earnings Communication:**
- Responsible: Investor Relations Manager
- Accountable: CFO
- Consulted: CEO, Legal, External Auditors, Communications
- Informed: Board, employees (post-release), media relations team

**Social Media Campaign:**
- Responsible: Social Media Manager
- Accountable: Director of Digital Communications
- Consulted: Brand team, legal (if regulated industry), product marketing
- Informed: Customer support team, sales team, executive leadership

### RACI Governance Principles

Ensure exactly one Accountable person per deliverable. Multiple accountabilities create confusion and delays. The Accountable person has veto authority and bears responsibility for quality, accuracy, and timeliness.

Minimize the number of Consulted stakeholders to prevent bottlenecks. Each consulted party adds cycle time to the communications production process. Establish service-level agreements for consultation turnaround times, typically 24-48 hours for standard communications and 2-4 hours for urgent or crisis communications.

Document the RACI matrix and review it quarterly. Organizational changes, new product launches, or expanded market presence may require RACI updates. Store the current RACI in a shared, accessible location such as the communications team wiki or project management tool.

## Stakeholder Analysis Tools and Techniques

### Primary Research Methods

**Stakeholder Interviews:** One-on-one conversations with key stakeholders to understand their information needs, preferred channels, current perception of the organization, and concerns. Use a semi-structured interview guide with open-ended questions. Record and analyze for themes.

**Perception Audits:** Systematic assessment of how different stakeholder groups perceive the organization, its leadership, products, and communications. Combine quantitative surveys with qualitative interviews. Benchmark against competitors where possible.

**Media Analysis:** Systematic review of media coverage to identify which stakeholders are quoted, which narratives are dominant, and which journalists cover the organization. Use media monitoring tools to track volume, sentiment, and share of voice.

**Social Listening:** Monitor social media conversations to identify stakeholders who are actively discussing the organization, their sentiment, influence metrics, and the topics that drive engagement.

### Prioritization Techniques

**Weighted Scoring:** Assign numerical weights to stakeholder attributes (power, interest, urgency, influence, alignment) and calculate composite scores. This enables objective prioritization when resources are constrained.

**Scenario Analysis:** Evaluate how stakeholder positions might shift under different scenarios (market downturn, product failure, leadership change, regulatory action). Identify stakeholders whose classification changes significantly across scenarios and develop contingency engagement plans.

**Network Centrality Analysis:** Use social network analysis to identify stakeholders who serve as bridges between groups, information hubs, or gatekeepers. These structurally important stakeholders may warrant higher engagement priority regardless of their individual power level.

## Continuous Stakeholder Monitoring

Stakeholder analysis is not a one-time exercise. Establish continuous monitoring processes including quarterly stakeholder map reviews, real-time media and social monitoring, post-campaign stakeholder feedback collection, annual perception audit cycles, and event-triggered reassessments following mergers, crises, leadership changes, or market shifts.

Track stakeholder relationship health through a simple dashboard that captures engagement frequency, sentiment trend, response quality, issue escalation rate, and advocacy behavior. Flag relationships that deteriorate below acceptable thresholds for immediate attention from senior communications leadership.
