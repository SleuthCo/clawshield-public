# Reputation Management

## Reputation as a Strategic Asset

Organizational reputation is the aggregate perception held by all stakeholders based on past actions, current behavior, and future expectations. Research by the Reputation Institute (now RepTrak) and others consistently demonstrates that reputation accounts for 25-40% of a company's market value, is the strongest predictor of stakeholder supportive behavior (purchasing, investing, joining, recommending), takes years to build and minutes to damage, and creates a "reservoir of goodwill" that provides resilience during crises.

The communications function is the primary steward of organizational reputation, though reputation is ultimately determined by organizational behavior, not communications alone. Communications can amplify positive reputation signals, mitigate negative signals, and shape the narrative context in which stakeholders evaluate the organization — but it cannot substitute for genuine organizational performance and integrity.

## Online Reputation Monitoring

### Monitoring Infrastructure

Establish comprehensive monitoring across all channels where stakeholders discuss, review, or evaluate the organization.

**Media Monitoring:** Track coverage across online news, print, broadcast, and podcast mentions using tools such as Meltwater, Cision, or Muck Rack. Configure alerts for organization name, executive names, product names, and key issue terms. Monitor both direct mentions and contextual references (industry articles where the organization should be but is not mentioned).

**Social Media Monitoring:** Track mentions, hashtags, and conversations across all major platforms. Use social listening tools such as Brandwatch, Sprout Social, or Talkwalker. Monitor not only brand mentions but also competitor mentions, industry conversations, and sentiment trends. Configure real-time alerts for volume spikes or sentiment shifts.

**Review Platform Monitoring:** Track and respond to reviews on Glassdoor (employer reputation), G2 and Capterra (product reputation), Google Business (local reputation), Trustpilot and BBB (consumer trust), and industry-specific review platforms. Each platform has different norms, user expectations, and response conventions.

**Search Results Monitoring:** Regularly audit first-page search results for the organization name, executive names, and product names. The first page of Google results is the de facto reputation dashboard for most stakeholders. Negative content on the first page has outsized impact.

**Forum and Community Monitoring:** Track discussions on Reddit, Hacker News, Stack Overflow, Quora, and industry-specific forums. These platforms often surface issues before they reach mainstream media.

### Monitoring Metrics

**Share of Voice (SOV):** The organization's proportion of total industry conversation. Track SOV trends over time and compare against key competitors.

**Net Sentiment Score:** The ratio of positive to negative mentions, calculated as (positive - negative) / total * 100. Track overall sentiment and sentiment by topic, audience segment, and channel.

**Velocity and Volume:** The rate and volume of mentions. Sudden spikes may indicate emerging issues or viral content. Establish baseline volume levels to enable anomaly detection.

**Influence-Weighted Mentions:** Not all mentions are equal. Weight mentions by the source's reach, credibility, and audience alignment. A negative mention in the Wall Street Journal is more significant than a negative tweet from an account with 50 followers.

## Negative Press Response

### Assessment Framework

When negative press appears, assess before reacting. Evaluate the accuracy of the reporting (factual errors versus unfavorable-but-accurate coverage), the reach and influence of the publication, the likelihood of amplification by other outlets, the current news cycle context (will this story gain or lose traction?), and the potential for the story to evolve or escalate.

### Response Options

**No Response:** Appropriate when the coverage is a minor mention in a low-reach outlet, the story is factually accurate and will quickly cycle out of the news, responding would amplify a story that would otherwise receive minimal attention, or the coverage represents legitimate opinion or criticism that does not require correction.

**Correction Request:** Appropriate when the article contains demonstrable factual errors. Contact the journalist directly with specific corrections and supporting evidence. Remain professional and collaborative — journalists generally want to be accurate. Document the correction request in case the error is not corrected.

**Statement Response:** When the coverage is significant and a response is warranted, provide a statement that addresses the specific concerns raised without repeating the negative framing. Lead with the organization's perspective and evidence, not a point-by-point rebuttal of the article.

**Proactive Counter-Narrative:** When negative coverage reflects a broader narrative that needs addressing, develop a proactive campaign to shift the conversation. This may include placing positive stories with other outlets, publishing the organization's perspective through owned channels, securing third-party validators who can provide alternative viewpoints, and creating content that provides context missing from the negative coverage.

**Legal Action:** As a last resort, when coverage is demonstrably defamatory and corrections have been refused. Legal action against media outlets is extremely high-risk from a reputation perspective — it draws more attention to the negative coverage and can be perceived as an attempt to suppress legitimate journalism. Consult closely with legal counsel and consider the reputational cost-benefit carefully.

### Negative Press Response Protocol

**Hour 1:** Identify and assess the coverage. Alert relevant executives. Determine response strategy. Prepare draft response if response is warranted.

**Hours 2-4:** Executive review and approval of response strategy. Legal review of any public statement. Contact journalist (if correction is needed). Monitor for pickup by other outlets.

**Hours 4-24:** Execute response strategy. Monitor impact of response. Prepare internal communications if employees are likely to see the coverage. Brief customer-facing teams if relevant.

**Days 2-7:** Monitor for follow-up coverage. Assess whether the story has legs or has cycled out. Implement sustaining strategy if the narrative persists.

## Social Media Crisis Management

### Social Media Crisis Identification

A social media crisis is distinguished from normal negative social media activity by velocity (rapid increase in negative mentions), volume (total mentions significantly exceeding baseline), sentiment (overwhelmingly negative), influence (prominent accounts amplifying the narrative), coordinated behavior (organized campaigns or hashtags targeting the organization), and mainstream media bridge (when social media conversation crosses into traditional media coverage).

### Social Media Response Protocol

**Assess and Classify:** Determine whether the situation is an isolated complaint, a trending negative conversation, or a full social media crisis. Each requires a different response level.

**For Isolated Complaints:** Respond directly to the individual. Acknowledge the concern. Offer to resolve privately. Follow up publicly to demonstrate resolution.

**For Trending Negative Conversations:** Monitor closely. Prepare approved response messaging. Respond to the most visible and influential posts. Do not attempt to respond to every individual post — this is neither possible nor effective at scale. Address the root issue rather than individual symptoms.

**For Full Social Media Crises:** Activate crisis response protocols. Issue a statement through official social channels. Pin the statement to profile pages. Pause all scheduled content. Assign dedicated team members to monitor and respond. Coordinate with traditional media response. Consider whether a direct video message from leadership is appropriate.

### Principles for Social Media Crisis Response

Respond in the tone and format native to the platform. Do not issue legalistic press-release-style statements on Twitter/X. Acknowledge emotion without being defensive. Avoid corporate jargon — social media audiences expect human communication. Do not delete negative posts unless they violate terms of service (deletion is almost always detected and amplified). Do not argue with individuals — state the organization's position clearly and move on. Use the organization's account, not personal accounts, for official responses.

## Review Management

### Review Response Strategy

**Positive Reviews:** Thank the reviewer specifically. Reinforce the positive experience mentioned. Keep the response brief and genuine. Avoid using the response as a marketing opportunity.

**Neutral Reviews:** Thank the reviewer. Address any specific concerns raised. Offer to discuss how the experience could be improved. Demonstrate that the organization takes all feedback seriously.

**Negative Reviews:** Respond within 24-48 hours. Acknowledge the concern and express genuine regret for the negative experience. Avoid being defensive or dismissive. Offer to resolve the issue offline (provide a specific contact). Follow up to ensure resolution. Never question the reviewer's honesty publicly, even if the review appears questionable.

**Fake or Malicious Reviews:** Most platforms have reporting mechanisms for fraudulent reviews. Document evidence and submit through proper channels. Do not publicly accuse a reviewer of fraud — this creates a worse optic than the original review.

### Review Generation Strategy

The best defense against negative reviews is a strong base of positive reviews. Implement systematic review generation by identifying moments of high customer satisfaction and prompting review submission, making the review process easy (direct links, QR codes, follow-up emails), training customer-facing staff to encourage reviews, and responding to all reviews to signal that the organization is actively engaged on the platform.

### Glassdoor and Employer Reputation

Glassdoor reviews significantly influence talent acquisition. Monitor and respond to Glassdoor reviews with the same rigor as customer reviews. Respond as "Employer" with thoughtful, non-defensive responses. Address systemic themes through actual workplace improvements, not just communications. Encourage current employees to share authentic experiences (never incentivize or pressure reviews, which violates Glassdoor terms).

## Brand Recovery Strategy

### Recovery Phases

**Phase 1 — Containment (Weeks 1-4):** Stop the bleeding. Stabilize the situation. Demonstrate accountability and immediate corrective action. Maintain consistent, transparent communication.

**Phase 2 — Remediation (Months 1-3):** Implement substantive changes that address the root cause of the reputation damage. Communicate specific actions taken, milestones achieved, and ongoing commitments. Engage third-party validators (auditors, certifiers, independent reviewers) to verify changes.

**Phase 3 — Rebuilding (Months 3-12):** Gradually shift narrative from crisis response to organizational improvement. Highlight positive developments, customer success stories, and employee engagement. Increase earned media activity around positive themes. Reintroduce thought leadership and forward-looking communications.

**Phase 4 — Reinforcement (Ongoing):** Embed the lessons learned into organizational culture and communications. Monitor for regression. Maintain elevated stakeholder engagement. Use the recovery story itself as a demonstration of organizational resilience and values.

### Recovery Communications Tactics

Publish a detailed accountability statement that acknowledges what went wrong, takes responsibility, and outlines specific remediation steps. Provide regular progress reports on remediation efforts. Invite independent assessment and share results transparently. Feature affected stakeholders in recovery communications (with their permission). Invest in community, employee, and customer engagement programs that demonstrate renewed commitment. Do not attempt to rush past the crisis — premature declarations of "moving forward" ring hollow. Let the recovery timeline match the severity of the event.

### Measuring Recovery

Track reputation recovery through brand perception surveys (pre-crisis baseline vs. current), media sentiment trajectory, social media sentiment trends, customer retention and acquisition rates, employee engagement and retention metrics, investor confidence indicators, and search result composition for branded terms.

Reputation recovery typically takes 18-36 months for significant crises, with the trajectory depending heavily on the genuineness and visibility of remediation efforts. Communications alone cannot restore reputation — it must be backed by authentic organizational change.
