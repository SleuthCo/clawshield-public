# Crisis Response Playbook

## Crisis Classification System

### Tier 1 — Critical Crisis

A Tier 1 crisis poses an existential or near-existential threat to the organization. It involves immediate risk to human life or safety, massive financial exposure, severe regulatory action, or fundamental threats to organizational viability.

**Examples:** Workplace fatality, product causing serious injury or death, major data breach affecting millions of customers, CEO arrest or serious criminal allegations, environmental disaster, imminent bankruptcy, hostile takeover, and confirmed fraud.

**Response Parameters:** Activate full crisis team within 30 minutes. CEO serves as primary spokesperson. Board chair and legal counsel are engaged immediately. External crisis communications counsel is retained. Initial holding statement issued within 60 minutes. First substantive statement within 4 hours. 24/7 communications monitoring and response. Daily stakeholder updates minimum.

### Tier 2 — Significant Crisis

A Tier 2 crisis causes substantial reputational, operational, or financial damage but does not threaten organizational viability. It requires senior leadership involvement and dedicated crisis management resources.

**Examples:** Significant service outage affecting major customers, executive departure under negative circumstances, product recall, discriminatory behavior allegation against leadership, material financial restatement, major customer loss made public, regulatory investigation announcement, and significant litigation filing.

**Response Parameters:** Activate crisis team within 2 hours. Senior VP or C-suite executive serves as spokesperson (CEO involvement as appropriate). Initial holding statement within 2 hours. First substantive statement within 8 hours. Extended business hours monitoring. Stakeholder updates every 24-48 hours during active phase.

### Tier 3 — Moderate Issue

A Tier 3 issue is a reputational or operational concern that could escalate if mismanaged but is contained at the current level. It can typically be managed by the communications team with guidance from senior leadership.

**Examples:** Negative media article or investigative piece, social media controversy, minor service disruption, employee misconduct (non-executive), negative analyst report, customer complaint gaining social media traction, and competitive attack narrative.

**Response Parameters:** Assess within 4 hours. Communications director manages response. VP or C-suite briefed. Response strategy determined within 8 hours. Monitoring during business hours with alerts configured for escalation signals.

### Escalation Triggers

Define clear triggers that escalate a crisis from one tier to the next: media pickup velocity (number of outlets covering in a defined timeframe), social media volume and sentiment thresholds, customer or partner inquiries exceeding defined thresholds, regulatory or legal developments, employee concern volume through internal channels, and executive or board inquiry.

## Holding Statements

### Purpose of Holding Statements

A holding statement is a prepared, pre-approved response used in the immediate aftermath of a crisis before full facts are available. It acknowledges the situation, demonstrates organizational awareness and concern, and buys time for a thorough response without appearing evasive or unresponsive. Silence in the first hours of a crisis is interpreted as indifference, guilt, or incompetence.

### Holding Statement Template

"[Organization name] is aware of [brief, factual description of the situation]. We are actively investigating and are working with [relevant parties — authorities, experts, partners] to understand the full scope. [If applicable: The safety/security of our customers/employees/community is our top priority, and we are taking immediate steps to address the situation.] We will provide further information as it becomes available. For immediate inquiries, please contact [spokesperson name and contact]."

### Holding Statement Principles

State only confirmed facts — never speculate. Express appropriate concern proportional to the severity of the situation. Demonstrate action — "we are actively investigating" or "we have taken immediate steps." Avoid blame, even if another party is clearly responsible, until the investigation is complete. Do not estimate timelines for resolution unless there is high confidence. Do not use legal disclaimers or hedging language in the initial statement — this reads as defensive.

### Pre-Approved Holding Statements

Develop pre-approved holding statement templates for the most probable crisis scenarios. These should be reviewed and updated quarterly. Having a legal-approved template available saves critical hours in the early stages of a crisis. Templates should exist for data breach or cybersecurity incident, service or product outage, workplace safety incident, executive misconduct allegation, regulatory investigation, product safety concern, natural disaster affecting operations, and activist or protest action.

## Response Timelines

### The Golden Hour Framework

**0-15 minutes:** Crisis detected. Notify crisis team lead. Preliminary assessment of tier classification. Activate monitoring tools.

**15-30 minutes:** Crisis team lead assesses and classifies. Notifies appropriate executives. Activates crisis team (full or partial based on tier). Identifies facts known and facts needed.

**30-60 minutes:** Crisis team convenes (in-person or virtual). Review holding statement and adapt for the specific situation. Assign roles and responsibilities. Issue holding statement through appropriate channels. Begin social media monitoring and response protocol.

**1-4 hours:** Gather additional facts. Develop first substantive response. Brief spokespeople. Prepare internal communications. Engage external counsel or advisors if needed. Conduct first media outreach or response.

**4-24 hours:** Issue substantive statement with action steps. Brief board and senior leadership. Implement customer and partner communications. Monitor and respond to media coverage. Prepare for next-day coverage cycle.

**24-72 hours:** Maintain communication cadence. Provide updates as new information becomes available. Begin remediation communications. Assess and adapt strategy based on stakeholder response. Plan for sustained communications if the crisis continues.

## War Room Setup

### Physical War Room

Designate a specific room equipped with multiple screens for monitoring news and social media, a conference phone and video capability, whiteboard or digital display for status tracking and decision documentation, secure internet access, copies of crisis plans, contact lists, and holding statements, and power and charging stations for extended operations.

### Virtual War Room

For distributed organizations, establish a virtual war room using a dedicated crisis Slack or Teams channel (created from a template with pre-defined membership), a shared document for real-time status tracking and decision logging, a video conference link that remains open during active crisis operations, a social media monitoring dashboard shared on screen, and a secure file sharing location for draft statements, media clips, and evidence.

### War Room Roles

**Crisis Lead:** Oversees all crisis response activities. Makes final decisions on communications strategy and timing. Typically the CCO or VP of Communications.

**Spokesperson Manager:** Prepares and coaches designated spokespeople. Reviews all public statements. Manages media inquiry queue.

**Media Monitor:** Tracks all media coverage and social media conversation in real time. Identifies emerging narratives, misinformation, and escalation signals. Reports to crisis lead at defined intervals.

**Internal Communications Lead:** Manages all employee-facing communications. Coordinates with HR and people managers. Monitors internal sentiment.

**Stakeholder Coordinator:** Manages communications to customers, partners, vendors, regulators, and other non-media external stakeholders.

**Legal Liaison:** Reviews all public statements for legal risk. Advises on regulatory obligations and disclosure requirements.

**Decision Logger:** Documents all decisions, approvals, and communications actions with timestamps. This creates an audit trail for post-crisis review and potential legal proceedings.

## Spokesperson Preparation

### Spokesperson Selection

Select spokespeople based on the crisis tier and type. The CEO should be the spokesperson for Tier 1 crises and any crisis involving loss of life or fundamental organizational integrity. Subject matter experts should be available for technical questions. The spokesperson should have the authority to speak on behalf of the organization, the composure to remain calm under hostile questioning, and the credibility with the affected stakeholder audience.

### Spokesperson Briefing Protocol

Before any public appearance, the spokesperson should be briefed on all confirmed facts and the current assessment, the approved messaging and key points, the questions most likely to be asked and approved responses, the topics that are off-limits (with reasons), the current media and social narrative, and the logistics of the upcoming interaction (format, interviewer, audience, duration).

### Spokesperson Dos and Don'ts

**Do:** Express empathy before providing information. Stick to confirmed facts. Use plain, direct language. Acknowledge uncertainty honestly ("We don't yet have complete information, but here is what we know..."). Maintain calm, measured delivery. Make eye contact and speak with conviction.

**Don't:** Speculate about causes, fault, or outcomes. Use "no comment" (instead, explain why you cannot comment: "We're unable to discuss specifics during the active investigation"). Make promises that cannot be guaranteed. Show irritation or defensiveness. Use jargon, acronyms, or technical language. Repeat a journalist's negative framing in your answer.

## Media Management During Crisis

### Proactive Media Strategy

Control the narrative by proactively providing information rather than only responding to inquiries. Issue regular updates on a predictable schedule — media will wait for your next update if they trust your cadence. Provide visual assets (photos, video, infographics) to influence how the story is illustrated. Offer spokesperson access to reduce reliance on outside commentators who may be less favorable.

### Reactive Media Strategy

Monitor all incoming media inquiries and log them centrally. Triage inquiries by outlet reach, deadline urgency, and tone of inquiry. Respond to all legitimate media inquiries, even if the response is limited. Track which journalists are covering the story and their angles. Correct factual errors promptly — contact the journalist directly with evidence.

### Social Media Crisis Management

Assign dedicated social media crisis responders separate from the media relations team. Develop a triage framework for social media responses: correct misinformation, acknowledge concerns, provide links to official information, and escalate hostile or threatening posts. Reduce or pause scheduled social media content that may appear tone-deaf in the context of the crisis. Consider using platform features (pinned posts, story updates) to ensure official information is prominent.

### Post-Crisis Media Strategy

After the active phase, proactively pitch follow-up stories focused on remediation, lessons learned, and organizational improvements. Offer exclusive access to leadership for in-depth features. Use the post-crisis period to rebuild media relationships that may have been strained during the event.

## Post-Crisis Review

### After-Action Review Process

Conduct a formal post-crisis review within 2 weeks of the crisis resolution. Include all crisis team members, relevant leadership, and external advisors. Review the timeline of events and response actions. Assess what worked well and what needs improvement. Evaluate the effectiveness of communications across all channels and audiences. Identify gaps in preparation, protocols, or resources. Document findings and update the crisis plan accordingly.

### Metrics for Crisis Response Evaluation

Evaluate crisis communications performance using response time (time from crisis detection to first public communication), message consistency (degree of alignment across channels and spokespeople), media coverage sentiment trajectory (how quickly sentiment stabilized or improved), social media conversation volume and sentiment, stakeholder inquiry volume and resolution time, employee confidence (pulse survey post-crisis), and long-term reputation impact (brand tracking data 30, 60, 90 days post-crisis).
