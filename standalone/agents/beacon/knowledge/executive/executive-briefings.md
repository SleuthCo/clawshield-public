# Executive Briefings & Communication Formats

## Principles of Executive Communication

Executive communication serves a fundamentally different purpose than general organizational communication. Executives operate under severe time constraints, process vast quantities of information daily, and make consequential decisions under uncertainty. Communications directed at or prepared for executives must therefore prioritize brevity without sacrificing completeness, structure that enables rapid comprehension, clear recommendations rather than open-ended analysis, evidence-based assertions that withstand scrutiny, and explicit articulation of risks, trade-offs, and alternatives.

The most common failure in executive communication is burying the lead — placing the conclusion or recommendation after extensive background. Executives need to know the answer first and then decide how deeply to explore the reasoning.

## The One-Pager

### Purpose and Format

The one-pager is the workhorse format of executive communication. It condenses a complex topic into a single page (approximately 500-600 words) that an executive can absorb in 2-3 minutes. One-pagers are used for pre-meeting briefings, project status updates, opportunity assessments, issue escalations, and recommendation summaries.

### One-Pager Structure

**Title and Date:** Clear, descriptive title with preparation date and author.

**Executive Summary (2-3 sentences):** The single most important takeaway. If the reader stops here, they should understand the core message.

**Situation/Context (3-4 sentences):** Relevant background that frames the issue. Include only what is necessary to understand the recommendation. Avoid historical narrative unless it is directly relevant.

**Analysis/Key Findings (1-2 short paragraphs or a bulleted list):** The evidence, data, or reasoning that supports the recommendation. Use quantitative data where available. Highlight the most significant findings.

**Recommendation (1-2 sentences):** A clear, specific, actionable recommendation. State what you are asking the executive to decide, approve, or do.

**Next Steps (bulleted list):** 3-5 concrete actions with owners and timelines if the recommendation is approved.

**Risks/Considerations (optional, 2-3 bullets):** Material risks, dependencies, or trade-offs the executive should be aware of.

### One-Pager Best Practices

Use 11-12pt font with adequate margins. Do not reduce font size to cram more content — if it does not fit on one page, the content needs editing, not reformatting. Use white space strategically to aid scanning. Bold key figures and critical phrases. Avoid jargon and acronyms unless the audience is certain to know them. Include a "prepared by" attribution so the executive knows who to contact for questions.

## Decision Briefs

### Purpose and Format

Decision briefs are used when an executive or leadership team must choose between defined alternatives. They are more structured than one-pagers and typically run 2-3 pages. The format is designed to present options objectively while making a clear recommendation.

### Decision Brief Structure

**Decision Required:** State in one sentence exactly what decision is needed and by when.

**Background:** Provide the context necessary to understand the decision. Limit to one-half page. Reference supporting documents for deeper background.

**Options Analysis:** Present 2-4 options using a consistent evaluation structure for each:
- Option description (what it entails)
- Advantages (with supporting evidence)
- Disadvantages (with risk quantification where possible)
- Resource requirements (cost, time, people)
- Expected outcomes (with confidence level)

**Evaluation Criteria:** Explicitly state the criteria used to evaluate options. Common criteria include strategic alignment, cost, speed, risk level, scalability, and stakeholder impact.

**Comparison Matrix:** A table summarizing how each option scores against each criterion. Use a simple rating scale (High/Medium/Low or 1-5) with color coding if appropriate.

**Recommendation:** State the recommended option and the primary reasons for selecting it. Acknowledge the strongest argument for alternative options and explain why the recommendation is nevertheless preferred.

**Implementation Plan:** If the recommendation is approved, outline the immediate next steps (first 30 days), resource allocation requirements, key milestones and decision gates, and risk mitigation actions.

## Board Presentation Best Practices

### Board Communication Principles

Board members are governance-focused, strategic thinkers with limited time and broad responsibilities across multiple organizations. Board presentations must respect these constraints while providing sufficient information for fiduciary oversight.

**Lead with Strategy, Not Operations:** Board members care about strategic direction, competitive positioning, market dynamics, and risk management. Operational details should be available in appendices but not dominate the presentation.

**Quantify Everything Possible:** Board members expect data-driven presentations. Replace qualitative assessments with quantitative metrics wherever possible. "Customer satisfaction improved" becomes "NPS increased from 42 to 57, placing us in the top quartile of our peer group."

**Acknowledge Risks Transparently:** Boards do not respond well to exclusively positive narratives. Credibility comes from balanced presentations that identify challenges, quantify risks, and present mitigation strategies. A communications leader who only delivers good news will eventually lose board trust.

**Provide Competitive Context:** Frame all results in competitive context. Market share, share of voice, brand perception rankings, and competitive benchmarking demonstrate where the organization stands relative to peers.

### Board Presentation Structure

**Communications Dashboard (1 slide):** Key metrics at a glance — media sentiment, share of voice, major coverage highlights, social media performance, employee engagement scores, and crisis incidents.

**Strategic Narrative Update (2-3 slides):** How the communications function is advancing organizational strategy. Specific campaigns, initiatives, or programs tied to strategic objectives with results.

**Reputation and Risk Assessment (1-2 slides):** Current reputation standing, emerging risks, stakeholder sentiment trends, and proactive mitigation efforts.

**Competitive Landscape (1 slide):** Competitor communications activity, positioning shifts, and share of voice comparison.

**Forward Look (1-2 slides):** Planned initiatives, upcoming milestones, resource requirements, and anticipated challenges.

**Appendix:** Detailed metrics, campaign summaries, media coverage compilation, and supporting data.

### Board Communication Cadence

Quarterly board presentations are standard for the communications function. Between meetings, provide monthly written updates to the board secretary or relevant committee chair. Reserve ad-hoc communications for material events: major crises, significant reputation events, executive communications issues, or regulatory actions.

## Elevator Pitches

### The 30-Second Elevator Pitch

An elevator pitch is a concise, persuasive summary designed to spark interest in 30 seconds or less. In a communications context, elevator pitches are prepared for executives to use when asked "What does your company do?" or "What's new with the organization?"

**Structure:** Hook (attention-grabbing opening statement) + Problem (the challenge the organization addresses) + Solution (how the organization addresses it) + Proof (one compelling evidence point) + Ask (what you want the listener to do next).

**Example:** "Every enterprise generates more data than their teams can possibly manage. [Company] makes that data infrastructure invisible — our platform automates 90% of the pipeline engineering that used to consume entire teams. Companies like [Fortune 500 customer] have redeployed those engineers to product development, shipping features 3x faster. I'd love to show you a quick demo."

### The 60-Second Version

Extends the 30-second pitch with additional proof points, a brief competitive differentiator, and a more specific call to action. This version is appropriate for networking events, conference hallway conversations, and informal meetings.

### Pitch Development Process

Work with executives to develop pitches that feel natural to their speaking style. A pitch that reads well on paper but feels unnatural when spoken will not be used. Record executives delivering the pitch, provide feedback, and iterate until it flows conversationally. Update pitches quarterly to reflect current positioning, recent achievements, and strategic priorities.

## Speaking Points and Talking Points

### Speaking Points Structure

Speaking points are prepared guidance for executives appearing in media interviews, public forums, panel discussions, or internal meetings. They provide message discipline without scripting, allowing the executive to sound natural while staying on strategy.

**Format:** Each speaking point should include the key message (one clear statement), the supporting evidence (2-3 proof points), the bridge phrase (a transition that redirects to the key message if the conversation drifts), and anticipated questions with suggested responses.

**Bridge Phrases for Executives:** "What's really important here is..." / "The key point to understand is..." / "Let me put that in context..." / "What I can tell you is..." / "The bigger picture here is..." These phrases allow executives to acknowledge questions while redirecting to strategic messages.

### Speaking Points Development

**Research the Context:** Understand the specific event, audience, interviewer or moderator, other panelists, and likely topics. Speaking points must be contextually relevant.

**Align with Message Architecture:** All speaking points should derive from the organization's message house. Identify which pillar messages are most relevant to the specific context.

**Prepare for Adversarial Questions:** Identify the toughest questions the executive might face and prepare bridging responses. The executive should never appear evasive but should be equipped to redirect from hostile framing to constructive narrative.

**Include "Do Not Say" Guidance:** Explicitly identify phrases, topics, or framings the executive should avoid. This is particularly important in regulated industries, during litigation, or in competitive contexts where careless statements create legal or strategic risk.

### Verbal Briefing Protocol

For high-stakes appearances, supplement written speaking points with a verbal briefing session. Walk the executive through the key messages, practice responses to difficult questions, and conduct a brief rehearsal. For media interviews, conduct a mock interview with a communications team member playing the journalist. For board presentations, conduct a dry run with the full presentation.

## Executive Communication Coaching

### Communication Style Assessment

Assess each executive's natural communication style and build on strengths rather than imposing a uniform approach. Some executives are natural storytellers — equip them with compelling narratives. Others are data-driven — provide them with powerful statistics and frameworks. Some are conversational — prepare them for dialogue-based formats. Others are structured — create detailed outlines they can follow.

### Ongoing Development Areas

Work with executives to continuously improve clarity and conciseness (eliminating filler words and tangential detail), message discipline (staying on point under pressure), audience awareness (adapting style for different stakeholders), storytelling ability (using narrative to make data memorable), and presence and delivery (body language, pacing, eye contact, vocal variety for in-person and video appearances).

### Executive Visibility Strategy

Develop an annual executive visibility plan that identifies the target audiences each executive should reach, the channels and formats best suited to each executive's strengths, a calendar of speaking opportunities, media appearances, and content contributions, metrics for evaluating executive visibility impact, and coordination protocols to ensure executive communications reinforce rather than fragment the organizational narrative.
