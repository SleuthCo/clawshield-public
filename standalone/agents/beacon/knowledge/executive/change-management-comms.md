# Change Management Communications

## The Communications Function in Change Management

Communications is the connective tissue of change management. No organizational change — whether a restructuring, technology implementation, merger, cultural transformation, or process change — succeeds without effective communication. Research consistently shows that poor communication is the leading cause of change initiative failure, with studies by Prosci and McKinsey finding that projects with effective change communication are 6x more likely to meet objectives.

The communications function serves change management in several ways: building awareness of why change is necessary, creating understanding of what the change entails, managing emotional responses to change, equipping managers to cascade information, reinforcing new behaviors and norms, and providing feedback channels that surface resistance and enable course correction.

## Prosci ADKAR Model Communications

### The ADKAR Framework

The ADKAR model (developed by Prosci) identifies five sequential building blocks that an individual must achieve for change to be successful: Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, and Reinforcement. Communications strategy must address each stage with distinct messaging and channels.

### Awareness Stage Communications

**Objective:** Ensure all affected stakeholders understand that change is coming and why it is necessary. Without awareness of the need for change, individuals will resist or ignore subsequent change activities.

**Key Messages:** What is changing and what is driving the change (market forces, competitive pressure, regulatory requirements, strategic opportunity). Why the status quo is no longer viable. What the consequences of not changing would be. Who is affected and what the general timeline looks like.

**Channels and Tactics:** CEO or senior leader announcement (all-hands meeting, video message, written communication). Manager talking points for team-level discussions. FAQ document addressing anticipated questions. Intranet landing page serving as the single source of truth. Town hall Q&A session for open dialogue.

**Common Pitfalls:** Announcing change without explaining the rationale (creating fear and speculation). Using corporate jargon that obscures the real impact. Communicating once and assuming awareness is achieved — repetition across multiple channels is necessary. Failing to acknowledge the emotional impact of the change.

### Desire Stage Communications

**Objective:** Build personal motivation among stakeholders to participate in and support the change. Awareness alone is insufficient — individuals must also want the change to succeed.

**Key Messages:** What is in it for the individual (WIIFM). How the change aligns with personal and professional goals. What the cost of resistance is (for the individual and the organization). What support is available during the transition. Stories from early adopters or peers who have embraced the change.

**Channels and Tactics:** Manager one-on-one conversations (the most influential channel for building desire). Peer testimonials and champion networks. Interactive workshops where employees explore the benefits of change. Visible executive commitment and role modeling. Recognition of early adopters.

**Common Pitfalls:** Assuming that awareness automatically creates desire. Ignoring legitimate concerns and emotions. Relying exclusively on top-down communication without peer influence. Failing to address WIIFM for each stakeholder group.

### Knowledge Stage Communications

**Objective:** Provide stakeholders with the information they need to implement the change — new processes, systems, behaviors, or skills.

**Key Messages:** Specific instructions for what to do differently. Training availability and schedules. Resources and tools available. Where to get help and answers. Timeline for implementation milestones.

**Channels and Tactics:** Formal training programs and workshops. Step-by-step guides and job aids. Video tutorials and demonstrations. Knowledge base articles and FAQs. Help desk and support channels. Manager coaching sessions.

### Ability Stage Communications

**Objective:** Support stakeholders as they put knowledge into practice, addressing barriers and building confidence through actual performance.

**Key Messages:** Encouragement and normalization of the learning curve. Success stories and progress updates. Resources for troubleshooting common challenges. Feedback mechanisms for reporting issues. Adjustments being made based on employee input.

**Channels and Tactics:** Regular check-in communications from managers. Progress dashboards and milestone celebrations. Open office hours with change team and subject matter experts. Coaching and mentoring programs. Feedback surveys and pulse checks.

### Reinforcement Stage Communications

**Objective:** Sustain the change by recognizing adoption, celebrating results, and preventing regression to old behaviors.

**Key Messages:** Results achieved through the change (quantified where possible). Recognition of teams and individuals who have embraced the change. Ongoing commitment to the new way of working. Continuous improvement opportunities. Connection between the change and broader organizational success.

**Channels and Tactics:** Recognition programs and awards. Success story features in internal communications. Metrics dashboards showing adoption and impact. Executive reinforcement messages. Integration of new behaviors into performance management systems.

## Kotter's 8-Step Change Communications Plan

### Step 1: Create Urgency

Communications must establish a compelling case for change that makes maintaining the status quo feel more dangerous than changing. Present data on competitive threats, market shifts, or internal performance gaps. Share customer feedback that highlights unmet needs. Use storytelling to make the urgency emotionally tangible. Avoid manufacturing false urgency — credibility depends on honest assessment.

### Step 2: Build a Guiding Coalition

Communicate the formation of the change leadership team. Identify visible, credible leaders across the organization who will champion the change. Communicate their involvement and commitment publicly. Ensure coalition members are equipped with consistent messaging and prepared to advocate in their spheres of influence.

### Step 3: Form a Strategic Vision

Develop and communicate a clear, compelling vision of the future state. The vision should be imaginable (conveys a picture of the future), desirable (appeals to stakeholders' interests), feasible (comprises realistic goals), focused (provides clear direction for decisions), flexible (allows for alternative responses as conditions change), and communicable (can be explained in five minutes or less).

### Step 4: Enlist a Volunteer Army

Communicate the vision broadly and frequently. Kotter emphasizes that the vision should be communicated 10x more than leaders think is necessary. Use every available channel. Ensure all communications connect back to the vision. Empower employees to share the vision in their own words. Create mechanisms for bottom-up communication and idea contribution.

### Step 5: Enable Action by Removing Barriers

Communicate the removal of obstacles to change. When barriers are identified (outdated processes, misaligned incentives, unsupportive systems), publicly address their removal. This demonstrates leadership commitment and builds momentum. Communicate structural changes that enable the new ways of working.

### Step 6: Generate Short-Term Wins

Identify and aggressively communicate early successes. Short-term wins provide evidence that the change is working, reward change agents, and build momentum. Communicate wins broadly and quickly. Connect each win to the broader change vision. Recognize the individuals and teams responsible.

### Step 7: Sustain Acceleration

Communicate ongoing progress while maintaining urgency. Avoid declaring victory prematurely. Use the credibility from short-term wins to address bigger challenges. Communicate the evolution of the change initiative as it tackles deeper, more complex issues.

### Step 8: Institute Change

Communicate how the change is now embedded in organizational culture. Update formal communications (onboarding materials, policies, brand guidelines) to reflect the new state. Establish ongoing measurement and reporting that reinforces the permanence of the change.

## Resistance Management Messaging

### Understanding Resistance

Resistance to change is a natural, predictable human response — not a character flaw. Effective communications acknowledges resistance with empathy rather than dismissing it. Common sources of resistance include fear of job loss or role change, loss of competence or status, increased workload during transition, disagreement with the direction of change, lack of trust in leadership, change fatigue from too many simultaneous initiatives, and inadequate information or understanding.

### Resistance Response Strategies

**Cognitive Resistance (they don't understand):** Provide clearer, more detailed information. Offer multiple explanations through different channels and formats. Create opportunities for questions and dialogue.

**Emotional Resistance (they're afraid or angry):** Acknowledge emotions explicitly. Provide forums for expressing concerns. Connect with empathetic messaging that validates the difficulty of change. Share stories from others who have navigated similar transitions.

**Behavioral Resistance (they won't participate):** Identify specific behaviors that indicate resistance. Address through manager conversations. Provide additional support and training. Clarify consequences of sustained non-participation (as a last resort, not a first response).

### Manager Communication Toolkit for Resistance

Equip managers with conversation guides for common resistance scenarios, listening techniques for understanding root causes, escalation pathways for resistance they cannot address, and regular updates and FAQs so they can answer team questions confidently.

## M&A Communications

### Pre-Announcement Phase

Before a merger or acquisition is publicly announced, communications planning must occur under strict confidentiality. Develop holding statements, employee notification plans, customer communication strategies, and media response protocols. Identify spokespeople and prepare them with approved messaging. Coordinate with legal counsel on disclosure requirements and timing.

### Announcement Day Communications

**Sequencing:** Board approval, regulatory filings (if required), employee notification (before or simultaneously with external announcement), press release, customer communications, partner and vendor notifications, media briefings, analyst calls, and social media.

**Employee Communication:** Employees should hear about the transaction from their own leadership before external media. The announcement should address what is happening (the transaction), why it is happening (strategic rationale), what it means for employees (to the extent known), what happens next (process and timeline), and where to get more information and ask questions.

**Customer Communication:** Reassure customers that service continuity is a top priority. Address specific concerns relevant to each customer segment. Provide a direct contact for questions. Avoid making promises about future product or service decisions that have not yet been made.

**Media Communication:** Issue a joint press release from both organizations. Prepare spokespeople from both organizations with aligned messaging. Conduct a joint media briefing or conference call. Anticipate and prepare for questions about job cuts, culture clashes, regulatory approval, and customer impact.

### Integration Phase Communications

**Day 1-30 (Orientation):** Welcome communications, organizational structure announcements, interim leadership designation, cultural integration principles, and immediate operational continuity assurances.

**Day 30-90 (Foundation):** Integration team updates, combined organizational vision, brand and naming decisions, policy alignment announcements, and early win celebrations.

**Day 90-365 (Execution):** Ongoing integration milestones, cultural integration programming, combined company achievements, and anniversary communications celebrating the merger.

### Communication Principles for M&A

Communicate early and often — silence creates anxiety and speculation. Be honest about what is known and what is not yet decided. Acknowledge the emotional impact, particularly for the acquired organization. Maintain communication parity — both legacy organizations should receive equitable attention and respect. Use consistent terminology — decide whether to call it a merger, acquisition, or combination and be consistent. Celebrate both organizations' histories and contributions while building excitement for the combined future.
