## Thematic Analysis

Thematic analysis is a method for identifying, analyzing, and reporting patterns (themes) within qualitative data. Braun and Clarke's (2006) six-phase approach is the most widely used framework.

**Phase 1 — Familiarization**: Immerse yourself in the data. Read transcripts, field notes, or documents multiple times. Listen to audio recordings. Note initial ideas, questions, and connections. This phase cannot be shortcut — deep familiarity with the data is essential for quality analysis.

**Phase 2 — Generating initial codes**: Systematically identify interesting features of the data. Codes are the smallest units of analysis — they label a segment of data that is potentially relevant to the research question. Code inclusively (capture context around the key idea). Code everything that might be relevant — it is easier to discard codes later than to re-read all data. Use descriptive codes (summarize content), interpretive codes (go beyond description to attribute meaning), and pattern codes (identify themes or explanations).

**Phase 3 — Searching for themes**: Collate codes into potential themes. A theme captures something important about the data in relation to the research question and represents a patterned response or meaning. Themes are not determined by frequency alone — a theme may appear in only a few data sources but capture something crucial. Use visual methods: mind maps, affinity diagrams, or thematic maps to explore relationships between codes and themes.

**Phase 4 — Reviewing themes**: Check themes against the coded data extracts and the full dataset. Does the theme work in relation to the extracts? Is there enough data to support the theme? Is the theme internally coherent? Are the boundaries between themes clear? This phase may involve splitting, merging, or discarding themes.

**Phase 5 — Defining and naming themes**: Write a detailed narrative for each theme, identifying its essence and scope. Each theme should have a clear, concise name that conveys its meaning. Identify sub-themes where appropriate. Each theme should tell a coherent story, and together the themes should tell the overall story of the data.

**Phase 6 — Producing the report**: Weave together the analytic narrative with data extracts. Extracts should be vivid, illustrative, and clearly connected to the analytic point. Go beyond description to interpretation — explain what the themes mean in relation to the research question, existing literature, and theoretical frameworks.

**Reflexive vs. codebook thematic analysis**: Braun and Clarke distinguish reflexive thematic analysis (organic, researcher-driven, themes developed through interpretation) from codebook approaches (structured, predetermined coding frame, sometimes applied by multiple coders with reliability metrics). Reflexive TA treats the researcher's subjectivity as a resource rather than a bias to be eliminated.

## Grounded Theory

Grounded theory generates theory from data through systematic, iterative analysis. Developed by Glaser and Strauss (1967), it has evolved into three main variants: Glaserian (classic), Straussian (systematic), and constructivist (Charmaz).

**Core process — constant comparative method**: Continuously compare data with data, codes with codes, and emerging categories with new data. This iterative comparison drives theoretical development. As you code new data, compare it with existing codes and categories, asking: Is this similar to or different from what I have already seen? What new dimension or property does this reveal?

**Open coding**: Break data into discrete segments and assign conceptual labels. Ask: What is happening here? What is this an instance of? What category does this represent? Group related codes into higher-level categories. Each category should have defined properties (characteristics) and dimensions (range of variation along those properties).

**Axial coding** (Straussian variant): Reassemble data fractured during open coding by identifying relationships between categories. The paradigm model organizes categories around: causal conditions, phenomenon, context, intervening conditions, action/interaction strategies, and consequences. This provides a structured framework for understanding how categories relate.

**Selective coding**: Identify the core category — the central phenomenon around which all other categories integrate. Systematically relate subsidiary categories to the core category. Develop the storyline that integrates the theory. The resulting theory should explain the central process or pattern in the data.

**Theoretical sampling**: After initial data collection and analysis, sampling decisions are driven by the emerging theory. Select new participants, settings, or data sources that will help develop, refine, or test the emerging categories. Theoretical sampling continues until theoretical saturation — new data no longer produce new conceptual insights.

**Memo writing**: Memos are the intermediate analytical step between coding and writing the final theory. Write memos throughout the research process to capture: ideas about codes and categories, comparisons between data, theoretical connections, methodological decisions, and questions for further investigation. Memos become the building blocks of the final written theory.

## Content Analysis

Content analysis systematically classifies and quantifies textual, visual, or audio data. It can be quantitative (counting frequencies and patterns) or qualitative (interpreting meanings and themes).

**Quantitative content analysis**: Define the unit of analysis (word, sentence, paragraph, article, image). Develop a coding scheme with clear, mutually exclusive, and exhaustive categories. Operationalize each category with explicit coding rules and examples. Train coders and test inter-coder reliability before coding the full dataset. Analyze frequencies, co-occurrences, and patterns using descriptive and inferential statistics.

**Qualitative content analysis (Mayring)**: More interpretive than quantitative content analysis. Three approaches: (1) Summarizing — reducing material while preserving essential content through paraphrasing and abstraction. (2) Explication — adding context to ambiguous passages by drawing on surrounding text and external sources. (3) Structuring — extracting and categorizing data using a theory-driven or data-driven coding frame.

**Directed content analysis**: Start with an existing theory or prior research to develop initial coding categories. Data that does not fit existing categories generates new categories. This approach extends or validates a theory rather than building one from scratch.

**Conventional content analysis**: Categories emerge from the data rather than being imposed. Similar to inductive thematic analysis but typically more structured, with a focus on frequency and distribution of categories.

**Coding scheme development**: Begin with a pilot sample. Define categories based on the research question and, if applicable, prior theory. For each category, provide: a clear definition, inclusion and exclusion criteria, typical and borderline examples. Test the scheme on a training set, revise based on difficulties encountered, and then apply to the full dataset.

## Discourse Analysis

Discourse analysis examines how language constructs social reality, power relations, identity, and knowledge. Multiple traditions exist, differing in focus and theoretical foundations.

**Critical Discourse Analysis (CDA)**: Fairclough's three-dimensional framework analyzes: (1) text (linguistic features — vocabulary, grammar, cohesion, text structure), (2) discursive practice (how text is produced, distributed, and consumed), and (3) social practice (the broader social context, power relations, and ideological effects). CDA is explicitly political — it aims to reveal how discourse reproduces or challenges power asymmetries.

**Foucauldian Discourse Analysis (FDA)**: Focuses on how discourses (systems of meaning) construct objects, subjects, and knowledge. Identifies discursive resources (how topics are talked about), subject positions (roles made available by discourse), and power effects (what becomes possible or impossible through discourse). Less focused on linguistic detail than CDA; more focused on historical and institutional context.

**Discursive Psychology**: Studies how psychological concepts (attitudes, emotions, memories, identity) are constructed through talk and text. Focuses on: what actions discourse accomplishes (e.g., blaming, justifying, accounting), how speakers manage their stake and interest, and how psychological states are constructed rather than simply expressed.

**Analytical techniques**: Identify recurring patterns, metaphors, and rhetorical devices. Examine what is said and what is left unsaid (silences and absences). Analyze how subjects are positioned (who has authority, who is marginalized). Look for ideological assumptions that are taken for granted. Examine intertextuality — how texts reference and build upon other texts.

## Coding Frameworks and Approaches

Coding is the process of assigning labels to segments of qualitative data to facilitate organization, retrieval, and analysis.

**Deductive coding**: Start with a pre-determined coding frame derived from theory, prior research, or the research questions. Apply codes to the data. This approach is efficient when the research builds on established frameworks but may miss unexpected findings.

**Inductive coding**: Develop codes from the data itself, without imposing predetermined categories. Read data closely and generate codes that capture what is happening in each segment. This approach is responsive to the data but can be overwhelmed by the volume of codes generated.

**Hybrid approach**: Begin with a deductive framework (based on key concepts from the research question and literature) but remain open to inductive codes that emerge from the data. This balances theoretical grounding with sensitivity to the data.

**First-cycle coding methods** (Saldana): Descriptive coding (topic labels), in vivo coding (participant's own words), process coding (gerunds capturing actions), emotion coding (participant emotions), values coding (values, attitudes, beliefs), versus coding (conflicts and contradictions), evaluation coding (judgments of quality or significance), and holistic coding (single code for a large passage).

**Second-cycle coding**: Pattern coding (grouping first-cycle codes into themes or constructs), focused coding (selecting the most significant and frequent codes for deeper analysis), axial coding (exploring relationships between categories), and theoretical coding (conceptual models integrating categories into theory).

**Code management**: Maintain a codebook with: code name, definition, inclusion criteria, exclusion criteria, typical examples, and relationship to other codes. Regularly review and revise the codebook. Use hierarchical code structures (parent codes with child codes) to manage complexity. Document all coding decisions and the reasoning behind them.

## Intercoder Reliability

When multiple coders analyze the same data, intercoder reliability measures the consistency of their coding. It is a quality criterion for content analysis and some forms of thematic analysis.

**When to use intercoder reliability**: Essential for quantitative content analysis and codebook-based thematic analysis where the coding frame is applied by multiple people. Not appropriate for reflexive thematic analysis (Braun and Clarke), where the researcher's interpretive engagement is the analytical tool and imposing reliability metrics would be inconsistent with the epistemology.

**Reliability metrics**: Percent agreement (simple but inflated — does not account for chance agreement). Cohen's kappa (two coders — adjusts for chance agreement; above 0.80 is strong, 0.60-0.80 is moderate, below 0.60 is weak). Krippendorff's alpha (any number of coders, any level of measurement, handles missing data — the recommended metric). Fleiss' kappa (multiple coders, nominal data).

**Achieving reliability**: Start with a clear, well-operationalized codebook. Train coders together using sample data. Code a training set independently, then compare, discuss disagreements, and refine the codebook. Conduct a reliability test on a subset of data (10-20% of the full dataset). If reliability is below threshold, further train and retest. Only after achieving adequate reliability should coders proceed independently.

**Reliability in qualitative research**: Consensus coding (coders discuss every disagreement until agreement) produces consistent results but sacrifices independence. Negotiated agreement (independent coding followed by discussion of disagreements) is the most common approach. Audit trails (documenting all analytical decisions and their rationale) provide transparency even when formal reliability metrics are not used.

## Qualitative Data Analysis Software

CAQDAS (Computer-Assisted Qualitative Data Analysis Software) supports organization, coding, and retrieval but does not replace analytical thinking.

**NVivo** (QSR International): The most widely used CAQDAS. Features include hierarchical coding, matrix queries (cross-tabulating codes and data sources), word frequency analysis, text search, cluster analysis of codes, and visualization tools. Supports text, audio, video, images, PDFs, and social media data.

**ATLAS.ti**: Emphasizes network views for visualizing relationships between codes, quotations, and memos. Strong support for multimedia data. The margin-coding interface closely resembles manual annotation. Co-occurrence tables and code-document tables support systematic comparison.

**MAXQDA**: Combines qualitative and quantitative features (MAXDictio for dictionary-based content analysis, joint displays for mixed methods). Interactive document comparison, visual tools, and summary grids for cross-case analysis.

**Dedoose**: Web-based, designed for mixed-methods research. Accessible pricing for students and small teams. Supports qualitative coding, descriptive statistics, and cross-tabulation of qualitative and quantitative data.

**Open-source alternatives**: QualCoder (Python-based, free), Taguette (web-based, open-source, simple coding interface), and RQDA (R-based, integrates with R statistical analysis).

**Best practices**: Use software for data management, systematic coding, and retrieval — but conduct interpretation and theme development through your own analytical thinking. Software can reveal patterns you might miss (co-occurrence, frequency) but cannot judge significance or meaning. Maintain analytical memos within the software to document your evolving interpretation alongside the data.

## Quality Criteria for Qualitative Research

Unlike quantitative research (which uses validity and reliability), qualitative research has developed its own quality frameworks.

**Lincoln and Guba's trustworthiness**: Credibility (internal validity equivalent — member checking, prolonged engagement, triangulation, peer debriefing), transferability (external validity equivalent — thick description enabling readers to judge applicability), dependability (reliability equivalent — audit trail, reflexive journal), and confirmability (objectivity equivalent — audit trail showing data-to-interpretation chain).

**Tracy's eight criteria**: Worthy topic (relevant, significant, timely), rich rigor (sufficient data, appropriate methods, time in field), sincerity (self-reflexivity, transparency about choices and challenges), credibility (thick description, triangulation, member reflections), resonance (aesthetic quality, transferability, evocative representation), significant contribution (conceptual, practical, methodological, or moral), ethics (procedural, situational, relational, exiting), and meaningful coherence (achieves what it claims, uses methods consistent with its epistemology, connects literature, research questions, findings, and interpretations).

**Reflexivity**: The researcher's critical self-examination of how their positionality (identity, values, experiences, assumptions) shapes the research process and findings. Reflexive practice includes: keeping a reflexive journal, questioning interpretive choices, considering alternative interpretations, and being transparent about the researcher's role and perspective in reporting.

**Member checking**: Returning findings (themes, interpretations, or draft reports) to participants for verification and feedback. Participants may confirm, clarify, extend, or challenge the researcher's interpretation. Member checking enhances credibility but should not be treated as a definitive validity check — participants and researchers may legitimately interpret the same data differently.
