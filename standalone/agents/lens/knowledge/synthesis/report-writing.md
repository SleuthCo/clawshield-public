## Intelligence Report Formats

Intelligence products serve different purposes and follow different formats. Selecting the appropriate format depends on the audience, timeliness, and the nature of the intelligence.

**Intelligence Information Report (IIR)**: Reports raw intelligence from a single source. Contains: the information as reported, the source description and reliability assessment, the date and circumstances of collection, and any analyst comments on the information's significance. IIRs are factual — they do not contain analytical judgments or conclusions.

**Intelligence Summary (INTSUM)**: A periodic summary (daily, weekly) of significant intelligence developments. Contains: key developments since the last INTSUM, changes to existing assessments, new collection results, and items requiring attention or decision. INTSUMs are concise — typically 1-3 pages covering multiple topics. They serve as a current intelligence product keeping decision-makers informed.

**Intelligence Assessment**: An analytical product that provides judgment on a specific question. Contains: bottom line up front (BLUF), key judgments, supporting analysis, alternative hypotheses considered and why they were rejected, confidence levels, intelligence gaps, and implications for the consumer. Assessments represent the analyst's best judgment based on available evidence and reasoning.

**Intelligence Estimate**: A forward-looking assessment of how a situation is likely to evolve. Contains: the current situation, the forces and factors likely to drive change, the most likely trajectory, alternative scenarios, indicators to monitor, and confidence levels. Estimates are inherently uncertain — their value lies in structuring thinking about the future, not in predicting it precisely.

**Special Report / Deep Dive**: An in-depth research product on a specific topic. Contains: comprehensive background, detailed analysis, extensive sourcing, and thorough consideration of alternative viewpoints. Deep dives are the most thorough analytical product and may take weeks or months to produce.

## Executive Summaries

The executive summary is often the only part of a report that decision-makers read. It must convey the essential findings, judgments, and recommendations concisely and clearly.

**Structure**: Lead with the bottom line — the single most important finding or recommendation. Follow with 3-5 key judgments, each stated clearly and concisely. Briefly indicate the basis for the judgments (evidence strength, analytical confidence). Highlight implications for the consumer (what should they do with this information?). Close with the most important intelligence gaps or uncertainties.

**Length**: One page for reports under 20 pages. Two pages maximum for longer reports. If it cannot be summarized in one to two pages, the analyst does not yet understand the material well enough.

**Writing principles**: Use active voice. Use concrete, specific language. Avoid jargon unless the audience is technical. State conclusions directly — do not hedge unnecessarily, but do express appropriate uncertainty. Each sentence should add value — eliminate filler, redundancy, and tangential information.

**Common failures**: Summaries that read as a table of contents ("Section 2 discusses X, Section 3 analyzes Y") rather than conveying actual findings. Summaries that bury the key judgment in the middle of a paragraph. Summaries that include caveats and qualifications at the expense of clarity. Summaries that fail to state implications — telling the consumer what is happening but not why it matters.

## Key Judgments and Confidence Levels

Key judgments are the analytical conclusions that answer the intelligence question. They represent the analyst's assessment — not raw information, not consensus opinion, but reasoned professional judgment based on evidence.

**Writing key judgments**: State the judgment clearly and directly. Include the confidence level. Briefly indicate the basis (e.g., "based on multiple corroborated sources" or "based on limited but credible reporting"). Use calibrated probability language where appropriate.

**Confidence level framework**: High confidence means the judgment is based on high-quality information from multiple independent sources, strong analytical reasoning, and few or well-supported assumptions. Moderate confidence means the assessment is based on credible information and sound reasoning but contains gaps or assumptions that introduce meaningful uncertainty. Low confidence means the assessment is a plausible analytical judgment based on fragmentary information, significant gaps, or multiple untested assumptions.

**Probability language** (ODNI standard vocabulary): "Remote" — less than 5% probability. "Very unlikely" — 5-20%. "Unlikely" — 20-45%. "Roughly even chance" — 45-55%. "Likely" — 55-80%. "Very likely" — 80-95%. "Almost certain" — greater than 95%. Always pair verbal probability expressions with a numerical range to prevent misinterpretation.

**Distinguishing facts from judgments**: Clearly separate what is known (facts, verified information) from what is assessed (analytical judgments, interpretations). Use distinct language: "We know that X occurred" vs. "We assess that X is likely motivated by Y." This distinction is critical for consumers who need to know which parts of the assessment are well-established and which involve interpretation.

## Sourcing Standards

Proper sourcing enables consumers to assess the basis for analytical judgments and supports transparency and accountability.

**Source description**: Describe sources in enough detail for the consumer to assess their reliability and relevance, without compromising sensitive sources or methods. Indicate: the type of source (official data, expert analysis, media reporting, human source, signals intelligence, etc.), the source's access to the information (direct knowledge, secondhand, thirdhand), and the source's track record and potential biases.

**Multi-source corroboration**: Indicate when key judgments are supported by multiple independent sources. True corroboration requires sources to be genuinely independent — if two sources are both reporting the same original information, that is single-source intelligence, not corroboration. Document the independence of sources.

**Single-source warnings**: When a key judgment relies on a single source, flag this explicitly and assess the risk. Consider: What is the source's reliability? Does the source have direct access? Is the information consistent with other known facts? What would change if this source proves unreliable?

**Source limitation acknowledgment**: Honestly describe gaps and limitations in sourcing. "We lack reporting on X" is more useful than omitting the topic. Intelligence gaps are themselves important information for consumers — they reveal what we do not know and where collection should be directed.

## Bottom-Line-Up-Front (BLUF) Writing

BLUF is the fundamental principle of intelligence writing: state the conclusion first, then provide the supporting evidence and reasoning. This inverts the academic convention of building to a conclusion.

**Why BLUF**: Decision-makers are busy and often read only the first paragraph. If the conclusion is buried at the end, it may never be read. BLUF also enables consumers to decide how deeply they need to read — if the bottom line addresses their question, they may not need the full analysis. If they want to understand the reasoning, they continue reading.

**BLUF structure**: First sentence: the key judgment or finding, stated directly. Second sentence: the basis for the judgment (brief). Subsequent sentences: context and implications. Following paragraphs: supporting evidence, analysis, and alternative perspectives. This structure applies at every level — the report, each section, and each paragraph should each lead with its own BLUF.

**Examples**: Weak opening: "Several factors contribute to the current situation in Country X, including economic pressures, political instability, and external influences." Strong BLUF: "Country X is likely to experience a political crisis within six months, driven by unsustainable fiscal deficits and an increasingly fragmented ruling coalition."

**Balancing BLUF with nuance**: BLUF does not mean oversimplification. The bottom line should be stated directly, but qualifications, confidence levels, and alternative views should follow immediately. The art is in being both clear and honest about uncertainty.

## Analytic Writing Craft

Intelligence writing serves a specific purpose — informing decisions — and requires a distinct craft.

**Clarity and precision**: Use the simplest language that accurately conveys the meaning. Define technical terms when first used. Avoid ambiguity — words like "significant," "substantial," and "considerable" mean different things to different readers unless quantified. Specify time frames, quantities, and comparisons.

**Active voice**: "The analysis concludes that..." is stronger than "It has been concluded by the analysis that..." Active voice is clearer, more concise, and attributes responsibility for judgments.

**Evidence integration**: Weave evidence into the analytical narrative rather than presenting data dumps followed by interpretation. Each paragraph should make an analytical point supported by evidence. Cite sources inline or through footnotes. Do not make claims without visible evidence.

**Handling disagreement**: When analysts disagree, present the disagreement transparently. State the majority view and the dissenting view, with the evidence and reasoning supporting each. Do not paper over disagreements — they are analytically valuable because they reveal genuine uncertainty.

**Visualizations in reports**: Use charts, maps, timelines, and diagrams to convey information that is difficult to communicate in prose. Every visualization should have a clear analytical purpose — not decoration. Label axes, provide legends, and include explanatory captions. Reference every visualization in the text.

## Report Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any analytical product, evaluate it against quality standards.

**Content checks**: Does the report answer the intelligence question? Are key judgments stated clearly and upfront? Are confidence levels assigned and justified? Are alternative hypotheses considered and addressed? Are assumptions identified? Are intelligence gaps acknowledged? Are implications for the consumer stated?

**Evidence checks**: Is every factual claim sourced? Are sources described adequately for credibility assessment? Is the evidence sufficient to support the conclusions? Is diagnostic evidence (evidence that distinguishes between hypotheses) highlighted? Are single-source dependencies flagged?

**Logic checks**: Does the reasoning flow logically from evidence to conclusion? Are there logical fallacies? Are causal claims supported by evidence of causation (not just correlation)? Could the same evidence support a different conclusion? Is the conclusion proportionate to the evidence?

**Communication checks**: Is the writing clear, concise, and free of jargon? Does each paragraph have a clear purpose? Are visualizations clear and properly labeled? Is the format appropriate for the audience? Is the document properly structured with a clear BLUF, key judgments, and supporting analysis?

**Peer review process**: Have at least one other analyst review the product for: logical reasoning, evidence adequacy, alternative hypothesis consideration, writing quality, and factual accuracy. Peer review should be structured (using a checklist or rubric) rather than informal.

## Structured Reporting Templates

Templates provide consistency and ensure completeness. They should be adapted to specific organizational needs but share common elements.

**Standard assessment template**: (1) Title and classification. (2) Executive summary / BLUF (half-page to one page). (3) Key judgments (numbered, with confidence levels). (4) Scope and methodology (what the report covers, how the analysis was conducted). (5) Background (context necessary for understanding the analysis). (6) Analysis (the detailed reasoning, organized thematically). (7) Alternative analysis (hypotheses considered and why they were rejected or retained). (8) Outlook / Implications (what the findings mean for the consumer). (9) Intelligence gaps (what we do not know that matters). (10) Appendices (detailed data, methodology notes, source lists).

**Briefing note template**: (1) Issue (one sentence). (2) Bottom line (one paragraph). (3) Background (one to two paragraphs). (4) Analysis (two to four paragraphs). (5) Implications (one paragraph). (6) Recommended actions (bullet points). Total length: one to two pages.

**Warning product template**: (1) Warning (what is about to happen, when, and why it matters). (2) Indicators observed (evidence triggering the warning). (3) Assessment (likelihood and potential impact). (4) Recommended immediate actions. (5) Monitoring plan (what to watch for next). Total length: one page or less, prioritizing speed over comprehensiveness.
