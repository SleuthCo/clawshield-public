## Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)

ACH, developed by Richards Heuer for the CIA, is the most widely used structured analytic technique. It combats confirmation bias by requiring analysts to evaluate evidence against all plausible hypotheses simultaneously, rather than building a case for the favored hypothesis.

**Step 1 — Identify hypotheses**: Generate a comprehensive set of hypotheses that could explain the situation. Include hypotheses that seem unlikely — the purpose is completeness, not probability assessment at this stage. Involve multiple analysts to avoid individual blind spots. Aim for 5-7 hypotheses as a practical range.

**Step 2 — List evidence and arguments**: Compile all significant evidence, arguments, and assumptions relevant to the hypotheses. Include both evidence that supports hypotheses and evidence that would refute them. Evidence includes: direct observations, reports from sources, logical deductions, and the absence of expected evidence.

**Step 3 — Prepare the matrix**: Create a matrix with hypotheses as columns and evidence items as rows. For each cell, assess whether the evidence is consistent (C), inconsistent (I), or not applicable (NA) with the hypothesis. Focus on inconsistencies — evidence that is inconsistent with a hypothesis is more diagnostic than evidence that is consistent with it.

**Step 4 — Refine the matrix**: Review the matrix for completeness. Identify evidence items that are consistent with all hypotheses (non-diagnostic — they do not help distinguish between hypotheses). Consider adding hypotheses to account for unexplained evidence. Look for evidence that would be diagnostic but has not yet been sought.

**Step 5 — Draw tentative conclusions**: The hypothesis with the fewest inconsistencies is the most likely — not the hypothesis with the most consistent evidence (a crucial distinction that counteracts confirmation bias). Assess the sensitivity of the conclusion: would rejecting one or two pieces of evidence change the ranking?

**Step 6 — Analyze sensitivity**: Identify the evidence items whose assessment most affects the conclusion (the linchpin evidence). Reassess the reliability and interpretation of these critical items. If the conclusion depends heavily on one or two uncertain items, confidence should be low.

**Step 7 — Report conclusions**: State the most likely hypothesis and the confidence level. Identify the key evidence that drives the conclusion and the evidence that most challenges it. Describe what evidence or events would cause you to change your conclusion (indicators for monitoring).

**ACH limitations**: ACH works best when hypotheses are mutually exclusive. It can be cumbersome with many hypotheses or many evidence items. The binary (consistent/inconsistent) coding oversimplifies nuanced relationships between evidence and hypotheses. Analysts may unconsciously bias their consistency assessments. ACH should be used in conjunction with other techniques, not as a standalone method.

## Key Assumptions Check

A key assumptions check identifies and evaluates the assumptions underlying an analysis. Assumptions are beliefs taken for granted — they are often unstated and unchallenged, yet they shape analytical conclusions as much as evidence does.

**Identifying assumptions**: Assumptions exist at multiple levels: factual assumptions (taken-for-granted facts that may not be verified), analytical assumptions (beliefs about how the world works that shape interpretation), and value assumptions (priorities and preferences that influence judgment). Common categories: assumptions about the adversary's intentions, capabilities, or rationality; assumptions about the reliability of sources; assumptions about the stability of current conditions; and assumptions about the analyst's own objectivity.

**Evaluation process**: (1) List all assumptions identified by the team. (2) For each assumption, assess: What evidence supports this assumption? Could the assumption be wrong? What would change if it were wrong? How confident are we that the assumption holds? (3) Categorize assumptions as: well-supported (strong evidence, unlikely to be wrong), reasonable (some evidence, could be wrong but unlikely), or unsupported (little evidence, could easily be wrong). (4) Flag unsupported assumptions that significantly affect the conclusion — these are analytical vulnerabilities.

**Common dangerous assumptions**: Mirror imaging (assuming others think and act as we would), assuming rational behavior, assuming current trends will continue, assuming information is complete, assuming sources are reliable without verification, and assuming the absence of evidence is evidence of absence. Each of these is reasonable in some contexts but dangerous when applied uncritically.

**Integration with analysis**: Conduct key assumptions checks at the beginning of an analytical project (to surface starting assumptions), at critical decision points (when assumptions drive the direction of analysis), and before finalizing assessments (to identify vulnerabilities in the conclusion). Document assumptions in the final report so that consumers of the analysis can assess them independently.

## Indicators Validation

Indicators are observable events, conditions, or data points that signal whether a particular hypothesis, scenario, or event is unfolding. Indicators validation develops and tests these signals in advance.

**Developing indicators**: For each hypothesis or scenario, identify events or conditions that you would expect to observe if the hypothesis were true (positive indicators) and events or conditions that would suggest the hypothesis is false (negative indicators). Good indicators are: observable (can actually be detected), diagnostic (distinguish between hypotheses), timely (provide advance warning), and collectible (accessible through available collection methods).

**Indicator lists structure**: Organize indicators into categories: political, military, economic, social, informational, infrastructure (PMESII framework for geopolitical analysis). For each indicator, specify: what to look for, where to look, how often to check, and the significance of detection (confirming, disconfirming, or ambiguous).

**Validation and monitoring**: Test proposed indicators against historical cases — did these indicators precede past instances of the event? Establish baseline conditions so that changes can be detected. Set up systematic monitoring schedules. Track which indicators are observed, which are not, and what the pattern suggests.

**Avoiding indicator pitfalls**: Single indicators are rarely definitive — look for patterns of multiple indicators. Be aware of denial and deception — adversaries may create false indicators or suppress genuine ones. Distinguish between indicators and evidence (indicators suggest something may be happening; evidence confirms it).

## Scenario Analysis

Scenario analysis develops multiple plausible futures to inform decision-making under uncertainty. Unlike forecasting (which predicts the most likely outcome), scenario analysis explores the range of what could happen.

**Types of scenarios**: Exploratory scenarios examine how the future might unfold from present conditions (what could happen?). Normative scenarios describe desired futures and work backward to identify paths (what should happen?). Predictive scenarios estimate the most likely future (what will happen?). Each serves different analytical purposes.

**Scenario development methods**: The intuitive logics method (Shell/GBN approach) identifies critical uncertainties and develops scenarios around their intersection. The cross-impact method assesses how trends and events influence each other's probability. The morphological analysis method (see below) systematically combines features to generate scenario structures.

**Quality criteria for scenarios**: Plausibility (could this actually happen?), internal consistency (do the elements of the scenario fit together?), relevance (does the scenario address the key decision?), challenge (does it stretch thinking beyond the obvious?), and differentiation (are the scenarios genuinely different from each other?).

**Using scenarios for decision-making**: Test proposed strategies against each scenario — which strategies perform well across multiple scenarios (robust strategies) vs. which are optimized for one scenario but vulnerable in others? Identify early warning indicators for each scenario. Develop contingency plans for scenarios that pose the greatest risk. Review and update scenarios as conditions change.

## SWOT Analysis for Intelligence

SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) is adapted from business strategy for intelligence analysis of entities — whether organizations, countries, movements, or individuals.

**Intelligence-focused SWOT**: When analyzing an adversary or subject, Strengths and Weaknesses assess internal capabilities and vulnerabilities. Opportunities and Threats assess external conditions that could benefit or harm the subject. This perspective helps analysts understand the subject's decision calculus — what are they likely to do given their capabilities and environment?

**Strengths assessment**: Resources (financial, human, technological), capabilities (specialized skills, institutional knowledge), assets (physical infrastructure, intellectual property), relationships (alliances, networks), and reputation (brand, credibility, legitimacy). For adversaries, understanding strengths reveals what they can do and where they are most capable.

**Weaknesses assessment**: Resource gaps, capability limitations, structural vulnerabilities, internal divisions, leadership deficiencies, and reputational liabilities. Weaknesses reveal where the subject is most vulnerable and where pressure might be most effective.

**Opportunities assessment**: Environmental changes that the subject could exploit: political shifts, market changes, technological developments, alliances, and competitor/rival weaknesses. Understanding perceived opportunities helps predict the subject's likely future actions.

**Threats assessment**: Environmental changes that could harm the subject: regulatory changes, competitive pressure, technological disruption, internal dissent, and external opposition. Understanding threats helps predict defensive behaviors and potential desperation moves.

## Morphological Analysis

Morphological analysis, developed by Fritz Zwicky, is a systematic method for exploring the complete space of possible solutions or scenarios by decomposing a problem into independent dimensions and examining all possible combinations.

**Process**: (1) Define the problem or system to be analyzed. (2) Identify the key dimensions (parameters) that characterize the problem. Typically 4-7 dimensions are manageable. (3) For each dimension, identify the possible values (states, options, or conditions). (4) Construct a morphological box — a multi-dimensional matrix with dimensions as columns and values as rows. (5) Examine combinations of values across dimensions to identify possible configurations. (6) Apply consistency constraints — some combinations are logically impossible or mutually exclusive. Eliminate these to produce the feasible solution space. (7) Analyze the remaining configurations for interest, plausibility, or desirability.

**Application to scenario building**: Dimensions might include: political stability (stable, transitional, unstable), economic growth (high, moderate, negative), technological change (rapid, moderate, slow), and social cohesion (high, fragmented, polarized). Each combination of values defines a distinct scenario. Morphological analysis ensures that the scenario set is comprehensive and that no plausible combination is overlooked.

**Application to problem solving**: Dimensions might represent design parameters, strategic choices, or operational variables. Systematic exploration of the combination space can reveal novel solutions that would not emerge from incremental thinking.

**Cross-consistency assessment (CCA)**: The critical step that makes morphological analysis practical. For each pair of values from different dimensions, assess whether they can coexist. Mark impossible combinations. The remaining internally consistent configurations define the solution space. CCA is typically done by subject matter experts and can be computationally assisted for large morphological boxes.

## Diagnostic Techniques Integration

Structured analytic techniques are most powerful when combined in a coherent analytical workflow.

**Diagnostic sequence**: Begin with Key Assumptions Check to surface unstated beliefs. Use ACH to systematically evaluate evidence against multiple hypotheses. Apply Indicators Validation to establish monitoring frameworks for the leading hypotheses. Use Scenario Analysis to explore how the situation might evolve. Conduct a Pre-Mortem to identify what could go wrong with the leading assessment.

**Choosing techniques**: Match the technique to the analytical challenge. For evaluating competing explanations: ACH. For challenging consensus: Devil's Advocacy, Red Team. For exploring the future: Scenario Analysis, Morphological Analysis. For surfacing hidden factors: Key Assumptions Check. For monitoring: Indicators Validation. For creative thinking: brainstorming, outside-in analysis. Time and resource constraints may limit which techniques are feasible.

**Quality indicators**: A well-structured analysis will: explicitly state assumptions, consider multiple hypotheses, evaluate evidence against all hypotheses (not just the preferred one), identify diagnostic evidence that could distinguish between hypotheses, describe what would change the analyst's mind, and express conclusions with calibrated confidence levels.

**Common implementation challenges**: Analysts may resist structured techniques as bureaucratic overhead, especially under time pressure. Techniques require training to apply correctly. Results depend on the quality of inputs — garbage in, garbage out. Over-reliance on any single technique is dangerous. Techniques complement expert judgment but do not replace it.

## Confidence Assessment and Communication

Analytical confidence reflects the analyst's degree of certainty in the conclusion, based on the quality and quantity of evidence, the strength of reasoning, and the number of assumptions.

**Confidence levels**: High confidence — the conclusion is based on high-quality, corroborated evidence with strong logical reasoning and few assumptions. Moderate confidence — the conclusion is based on credible evidence and reasonable reasoning but with some gaps, uncertainties, or assumptions. Low confidence — the conclusion is an analytical judgment based on limited evidence, significant gaps, or uncertain assumptions.

**Factors affecting confidence**: Source quality and reliability, corroboration across independent sources, diagnosticity of evidence (how well it distinguishes between hypotheses), analytical rigor (structured vs. intuitive analysis), assumptions (number and vulnerability), and the breadth of information gaps.

**Communicating uncertainty**: Use calibrated probability expressions when possible (e.g., "we assess with moderate confidence — roughly 60-70% probability — that..."). Avoid vague hedging ("it is possible that...") which conveys no useful probability information. The ODNI's Analytic Standards use a defined vocabulary: "remote" (less than 5%), "unlikely" (5-20%), "even chance" (45-55%), "likely" (55-80%), "very likely" (80-95%), "almost certain" (greater than 95%). Explain the basis for the confidence assessment — what evidence and reasoning support it, and what would change it.
