## Weick's Organizational Sensemaking

Karl Weick's sensemaking model explains how people in organizations create meaning from ambiguous, equivocal situations. Sensemaking is not about accuracy or finding the "right" answer — it is about constructing plausible narratives that enable action.

**Seven properties of sensemaking**: (1) Grounded in identity construction — who I understand myself to be shapes how I interpret events. An analyst interprets situations differently based on whether they see themselves as an objective scientist, an advocate, or a problem-solver. (2) Retrospective — sensemaking is always about past events; we can only make sense of what has already happened, though we may do so very rapidly. We know what we think when we see what we say. (3) Enactive of sensible environments — our actions create the environments we then interpret. The questions we ask, the data we collect, and the relationships we build shape what we can observe. (4) Social — sensemaking is fundamentally a social process involving shared meanings, coordinated actions, and mutual influence. (5) Ongoing — sensemaking never starts from scratch; it is a continuous flow of experience that we punctuate and label. Interruptions to the flow trigger more conscious sensemaking. (6) Focused on and by extracted cues — we notice specific cues from the environment and use them as seeds for constructing larger meanings. Different cues lead to different sense. (7) Driven by plausibility rather than accuracy — people need a story that is reasonable and actionable, not necessarily one that is objectively correct.

**Sensemaking triggers**: Ambiguity (multiple interpretations are possible), uncertainty (information is insufficient), surprise (events violate expectations), and interruption (ongoing activities are disrupted). These triggers create a "cosmology episode" — a moment where the taken-for-granted understanding of the world collapses and must be reconstructed.

**Enactment-Selection-Retention (ESR)**: Weick's process model. Enactment: people take action that creates the raw material for sensemaking (asking questions, conducting experiments, initiating conversations). Selection: people apply mental models to select a plausible interpretation of the enacted experience. Retention: successful interpretations are stored for future use, becoming the mental models that guide future enactment and selection. The cycle is continuous and self-reinforcing.

**Implications for research**: The researcher's identity, assumptions, and actions shape what they find. Data does not speak for itself — it is always interpreted through mental models. Retrospective sensemaking means that analytical narratives are constructed, not discovered. Awareness of these dynamics helps researchers maintain intellectual honesty about the role of their own perspective in shaping conclusions.

## Dervin's Sense-Making Methodology

Brenda Dervin's Sense-Making methodology focuses on how individuals bridge gaps between their current understanding and the situations they face. It is primarily a communication research methodology but has broad applicability to understanding how people seek and use information.

**The metaphor of gaps and bridges**: Individuals move through time-space, encountering situations where their existing understanding is insufficient — gaps. To continue, they must construct bridges — using information, ideas, emotions, and actions to cross the gap. The process of constructing bridges is sense-making.

**The Situation-Gap-Use model**: (1) Situation: the context in which the person encounters the gap — their history, constraints, and environment. (2) Gap: what the person perceives as missing — the question, confusion, or need. (3) Use: how the person applies whatever bridge they construct — the help they derive, the actions they take, the emotions they experience.

**Micro-Moment Time-Line Interview**: Dervin's primary data collection method. Ask the participant to describe a specific situation step by step (the micro-moments). At each step, ask: What happened? What questions or confusions did you have? What did you do to address them? What helped or hindered you? What would have helped? This method captures the actual sensemaking process rather than post-hoc rationalizations.

**Applications to research design**: Understanding how your research audience (or your own team) makes sense of information helps design more effective information products. If people construct meaning through gap-bridging in specific situations, then research outputs should be designed to address specific gaps in specific situations, not to deliver context-free data.

**Implications for information systems**: People seek information in response to specific gaps in specific situations. Generic information retrieval (keyword search) may not match the actual gap. Understanding the situation and the nature of the gap leads to more effective information provision. This insight applies to designing research databases, knowledge management systems, and intelligence dissemination.

## Pirolli and Card's Sensemaking Loop

Stuart Card and Peter Pirolli developed a sensemaking model specifically for intelligence analysis, based on cognitive task analysis of how analysts actually process information. The model describes two interacting loops: a foraging loop and a sensemaking loop.

**The foraging loop (bottom-up)**: Analysts search for relevant information, extract relevant fragments, organize them into an "evidence file," and construct a preliminary schema (mental model) of the situation. This loop is driven by information needs — the analyst seeks data to fill gaps in understanding. Activities include: searching databases, scanning documents, extracting relevant passages, categorizing findings, and building evidence collections.

**The sensemaking loop (top-down)**: Analysts develop hypotheses, construct stories or explanations, evaluate evidence against hypotheses, and produce analytical products. This loop is driven by mental models — the analyst uses existing frameworks to interpret incoming information. Activities include: hypothesis generation, schema construction, evidence evaluation, narrative development, and report writing.

**Interaction between loops**: The two loops interact bidirectionally. Bottom-up information triggers revision of top-down mental models. Top-down hypotheses direct bottom-up information search. The analyst shifts between loops as needed — sometimes driven by incoming data (foraging mode), sometimes by the need to test a hypothesis (sensemaking mode). Effective analysis requires fluid movement between both modes.

**Cost structure of sensemaking**: Each activity in the sensemaking process has a cognitive cost. Reading a document is cheap; reorganizing an entire schema is expensive. Analysts naturally minimize cognitive effort, which creates predictable biases: preference for information that confirms existing schemas (less reorganization needed), resistance to changing established interpretations, and reliance on familiar sources and methods.

**Leverage points for improvement**: External representations (charts, link diagrams, timelines, matrices) reduce the cognitive burden of maintaining complex models in working memory. Structured analytic techniques provide scaffolding for the sensemaking loop. Information management tools support the foraging loop by facilitating search, extraction, and organization. Team-based analysis distributes the cognitive load.

**The notional model stages**: External data sources lead to a shoebox (collected raw data), then to an evidence file (organized and filtered data), then to a schema (mental model or framework), then to hypotheses (specific claims), and finally to a presentation or report. Each transition involves both bottom-up processing (data pushing the analyst to revise understanding) and top-down processing (the analyst's framework guiding selection and interpretation of data).

## Information Foraging Theory

Information foraging theory, developed by Pirolli and Card, applies optimal foraging theory from ecology to human information-seeking behavior. Just as animals optimize their foraging strategies to maximize energy gain per unit effort, people optimize their information-seeking strategies to maximize information value per unit effort.

**Information scent**: The perceived value of an information source based on available cues — titles, snippets, link text, descriptions, and other signals that hint at the content's relevance before the full content is accessed. People follow high-scent trails and abandon low-scent trails. In research, this means that how sources present themselves (titles, abstracts, metadata) strongly influences which sources are accessed, regardless of actual content quality.

**Information patches**: Information is distributed across patches (websites, databases, document collections, experts). Within a patch, there are diminishing returns — the most relevant information is found first, and continued search yields less and less value. The optimal foraging strategy involves: leaving a patch when the rate of new useful information drops below the expected rate of finding useful information by moving to a new patch (the marginal value theorem).

**Diet breadth**: Foragers must decide which information items to pursue and which to ignore. High-value items are always worth pursuing. Low-value items are worth pursuing only when high-value items are scarce. As the environment becomes richer in information (e.g., the internet), people become more selective — they narrow their diet to higher-quality sources because the opportunity cost of pursuing lower-quality sources increases.

**Implications for research practice**: Design searches to maximize information scent — use precise queries that generate results with clear relevance signals. Know when to leave a source — when diminishing returns set in, move to a new source rather than exhaustively mining a depleted one. Be aware that information scent can be misleading — high-scent sources may be low quality, and low-scent sources may contain critical information. Deliberately seek low-scent paths to avoid the bias of always following the obvious trail.

## Cognitive Task Analysis

Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA) is a family of methods for understanding the cognitive demands, strategies, and knowledge that experts use to perform complex tasks. CTA reveals the tacit knowledge that experts cannot easily articulate.

**Knowledge elicitation methods**: Structured interviews (critical decision method, concept mapping, knowledge audit), observation-based methods (think-aloud protocol, process tracing), and artifact analysis (examining documents, tools, and work products that experts create and use). Multiple methods should be combined for a comprehensive understanding.

**Critical Decision Method (CDM)**: A retrospective interview technique that probes expert decision-making in specific incidents. Steps: (1) Select a challenging incident. (2) Have the expert narrate the incident from start to finish. (3) Construct a timeline of key decision points. (4) Probe each decision point: What were you noticing? What were you thinking? What options were you considering? What would a novice have missed? What was the most difficult part? CDM reveals the cues, patterns, and mental models that experts use but rarely articulate.

**Think-aloud protocol**: The expert performs the task while verbalizing their thoughts continuously. The researcher records and transcribes the verbal protocol, then analyzes it for: cognitive strategies, knowledge used, decision points, and difficulties encountered. Think-aloud is valuable for capturing the process of reasoning, not just the outcome. Limitations: verbalization may alter the cognitive process, and some processes are too fast or automatic to verbalize.

**Applied to sensemaking research**: CTA methods can be used to study how analysts, researchers, and investigators actually process information and reach conclusions. The findings inform: tool design (building tools that support actual cognitive processes), training design (teaching the strategies that experts use), and process improvement (identifying cognitive bottlenecks and error-prone steps).

## Sensemaking in Teams and Organizations

Sensemaking at the group and organizational level involves additional dynamics beyond individual cognition.

**Distributed cognition**: In complex research tasks, no single individual holds all the relevant information or analytical capability. Cognition is distributed across people, tools, and artifacts. Effective teamwork requires: shared mental models (common understanding of the task and situation), transactive memory (knowing who knows what), and coordination mechanisms (processes for combining individual contributions).

**Common ground maintenance**: Teams must maintain shared understanding of: what is known, what is uncertain, what has changed, and what each team member is working on. Without common ground, team members may work at cross-purposes, duplicate efforts, or leave critical gaps. Regular briefings, shared information spaces, and structured communication protocols support common ground.

**Sensemaking breakdowns**: Teams and organizations can fail at sensemaking when: information is siloed (relevant data exists but is not shared), mental models are rigid (the organization cannot update its understanding despite contradictory evidence), authority dynamics suppress dissent (junior analysts defer to senior analysts even when they have better information), and communication channels are inadequate (critical information does not reach decision-makers in time).

**Enabling organizational sensemaking**: Create information environments that facilitate connection-making across data sources. Encourage devil's advocacy and structured dissent. Build collaborative tools that make individual analytical work visible to the team. Maintain institutional knowledge through knowledge management systems, after-action reviews, and mentoring relationships. Foster a culture where updating beliefs in response to new evidence is valued, not punished.

## Sensemaking Under Uncertainty and Complexity

**Uncertainty management**: Sensemaking under uncertainty requires: accepting that complete understanding may not be achievable, identifying and communicating the nature and degree of uncertainty, distinguishing between what is known, what is estimated, and what is speculated, and making decisions with incomplete information while remaining open to updating as new information emerges.

**Complexity and emergence**: In complex systems, outcomes emerge from interactions and cannot be predicted from analysis of individual components. Sensemaking in complex environments requires: pattern recognition rather than prediction, attention to weak signals and early indicators, tolerance for ambiguity, multiple concurrent hypotheses, and iterative experimentation.

**Narrative and sensemaking**: Humans make sense of complex situations through narrative — constructing stories that connect events causally and temporally. Narratives are powerful sensemaking tools but also sources of bias: we may force data into a coherent story when the reality is incoherent, and we may resist evidence that disrupts a satisfying narrative. Critical self-awareness about the narrative construction process is essential.

**Practical sensemaking heuristics**: Start by mapping what you know and what you do not know. Identify the most critical uncertainties. Seek information that could resolve the most critical uncertainties (high-diagnosticity evidence). Maintain multiple working hypotheses. Update continuously as new information arrives. Externalize your thinking (write, diagram, discuss) to offload cognitive burden and make reasoning explicit and reviewable.
