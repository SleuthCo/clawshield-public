## Logical Fallacies — Formal

Formal fallacies are errors in the logical structure of an argument. The conclusion does not follow from the premises regardless of content.

**Affirming the consequent**: If P then Q; Q is true; therefore P is true. Example: "If it rained, the ground is wet. The ground is wet. Therefore it rained." The ground could be wet for other reasons. This fallacy is extremely common in investigative reasoning — observing an effect and jumping to one particular cause without considering alternatives.

**Denying the antecedent**: If P then Q; P is false; therefore Q is false. Example: "If he is the thief, he would have been at the scene. He was not the thief. Therefore he was not at the scene." He could have been at the scene for other reasons. This fallacy leads investigators to dismiss evidence about subjects who have been "cleared" on other grounds.

**Undistributed middle**: All A are B; all C are B; therefore all A are C. Example: "All terrorists use encrypted messaging. This person uses encrypted messaging. Therefore this person is a terrorist." The middle term (uses encrypted messaging) is not distributed — it does not cover all members of the class. This fallacy is particularly dangerous in profiling and pattern-matching.

**False dilemma (false dichotomy)**: Presenting two options as exhaustive when other possibilities exist. "Either you support this policy or you support the enemy." In research, this manifests as framing questions with only two possible answers when the reality is more nuanced, or testing only two hypotheses when others are viable.

**Circular reasoning (begging the question)**: The conclusion is assumed in the premises. "We know the source is reliable because it provides accurate information, and we know the information is accurate because the source is reliable." In investigations, circular reasoning often hides within chains of inference that ultimately rest on the conclusion they are supposed to support.

## Logical Fallacies — Informal

Informal fallacies are errors in reasoning that stem from the content, context, or delivery of the argument rather than its formal structure.

**Ad hominem**: Attacking the person making the argument rather than the argument itself. Relevant when the person's credibility is genuinely at issue (e.g., a witness with a history of lying), but fallacious when used to dismiss evidence or reasoning based on irrelevant personal characteristics. In research, be cautious about dismissing findings because of the author's affiliations or reputation.

**Straw man**: Misrepresenting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack. In research debates, this often involves oversimplifying a complex argument, taking a claim out of context, or attributing a more extreme position than actually held. Always engage with the strongest version of opposing arguments (the principle of charity or "steel-manning").

**Appeal to authority**: Citing an authority figure as evidence for a claim outside their area of expertise, or treating any authority as infallible within their expertise. Expert opinion is legitimate evidence but should be evaluated based on: the expert's relevant qualifications, the degree of consensus among experts, the strength of the underlying evidence, and potential conflicts of interest.

**Red herring**: Introducing an irrelevant topic to divert attention from the original argument. In investigations, red herrings may be intentionally planted by adversaries or unintentionally created by analysts who follow interesting but irrelevant leads. Maintain focus on the specific intelligence question.

**Slippery slope**: Arguing that a small first step will inevitably lead to a chain of extreme consequences without evidence for each link in the chain. Valid when supported by evidence for each causal step; fallacious when the chain of consequences is assumed rather than demonstrated.

**Appeal to ignorance (argumentum ad ignorantiam)**: Claiming something is true because it has not been proven false, or false because it has not been proven true. "We found no evidence of wrongdoing, therefore no wrongdoing occurred." Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence — but the distinction between "no evidence found" and "evidence of absence" is critical in investigations.

**Hasty generalization**: Drawing a broad conclusion from insufficient evidence. A few cases do not establish a pattern. In research, this manifests as: generalizing from a small or non-representative sample, treating anecdotal evidence as definitive, or extrapolating trends from limited data points.

**Post hoc ergo propter hoc**: Assuming that because event B followed event A, A caused B. Temporal sequence is necessary but not sufficient for causation. Correlation does not imply causation — always consider alternative explanations, confounding variables, and reverse causation.

**Tu quoque (whataboutism)**: Deflecting criticism by pointing to similar behavior by others. "Country X violates human rights too." This does not address whether the original claim is valid. In research, analogies to other cases can be informative but should not substitute for evaluating the evidence in the current case.

## Argument Mapping

Argument mapping is the visual representation of the logical structure of arguments, making reasoning explicit and evaluable.

**Components of an argument map**: The contention (main claim) sits at the top. Premises (reasons) support or attack the contention. Sub-premises support premises. Objections attack premises or the contention. Rebuttals address objections. Each element is a distinct proposition (a statement that can be true or false).

**Co-premises vs. independent premises**: Co-premises work together to support a conclusion — removing any one weakens or destroys the argument (linked support). Independent premises each provide separate support — removing one leaves the others intact (convergent support). Distinguishing these structures is important for evaluating how much damage a rebutted premise does to the overall argument.

**Mapping tools**: Rationale (dedicated argument mapping software), MindMup, XMind, or even hand-drawn diagrams. The process of mapping itself — forcing reasoning into explicit propositional structure — often reveals gaps, hidden assumptions, and logical errors that are invisible in flowing prose.

**Evaluation of mapped arguments**: For each premise, assess: Is it true? (Evidence evaluation.) Is it relevant? (Does it actually support the conclusion?) Is the inference valid? (Does the conclusion follow from the premises?) What assumptions are hidden? What objections exist, and how strong are they?

## The Toulmin Model of Argumentation

Stephen Toulmin's model provides a practical framework for analyzing real-world arguments, which rarely conform to formal syllogistic logic.

**Claim**: The assertion or conclusion being argued for. In research, this is your finding, assessment, or recommendation. Claims should be specific, clear, and proportionate to the supporting evidence.

**Data (Grounds)**: The evidence, facts, or information that support the claim. Data may include: empirical observations, statistical results, testimony, documents, or established facts. The strength of the argument depends on the quality and relevance of the data.

**Warrant**: The reasoning principle that connects the data to the claim — why this data supports this conclusion. Warrants are often implicit and need to be made explicit for evaluation. Example: Data — "The suspect's fingerprints were found at the scene." Warrant — "People whose fingerprints are at a crime scene were likely present when the crime occurred." Claim — "The suspect was likely present at the crime scene."

**Backing**: Evidence or reasoning that supports the warrant itself. If the warrant is challenged, backing provides the underlying justification. In the fingerprint example, backing might include evidence about the reliability of fingerprint analysis and the persistence of fingerprints on surfaces.

**Qualifier**: An indication of the strength or certainty of the claim. "Certainly," "probably," "possibly," "likely." Qualifiers make the argument more honest and defensible by acknowledging uncertainty. Research conclusions should always include appropriate qualifiers.

**Rebuttal**: Conditions or circumstances under which the claim would not hold. "Unless the fingerprints were planted" or "unless the suspect was at the scene earlier for a legitimate reason." Identifying rebuttals strengthens the argument by demonstrating awareness of limitations and alternative explanations.

**Applying the Toulmin model**: For every key claim in your research, explicitly identify the data, warrant, backing, qualifier, and potential rebuttals. This structured approach reveals: where evidence is strong or weak, what assumptions connect evidence to conclusions, how certain the conclusion should be, and what could invalidate it.

## Critical Questioning Frameworks

Systematic questioning reveals the strengths and weaknesses of arguments and evidence.

**The Five Ws and How**: Who (is the source, who benefits, who is affected?), What (exactly is claimed, what evidence is presented?), When (was the claim made, when was the evidence collected?), Where (did the events occur, where was the source?), Why (is this claim being made, what motivation exists?), How (was the evidence obtained, how strong is the reasoning?). Simple but effective as a starting framework.

**Socratic questioning**: Six types that systematically probe reasoning. Clarification questions ("What do you mean by...?"). Probing assumptions ("What are you assuming?"). Probing reasons and evidence ("What evidence supports this?"). Questioning viewpoints and perspectives ("How might someone else view this?"). Probing implications and consequences ("If that is true, what follows?"). Questions about the question ("Why is this question important?").

**Analysis of Competing Hypotheses questioning**: For each piece of evidence, ask: Which hypotheses does this evidence support? Which does it weaken? Is the evidence diagnostic (differentiates between hypotheses) or non-diagnostic (consistent with multiple hypotheses)? This structured questioning prevents confirmation bias by forcing evaluation of evidence against all hypotheses, not just the favored one.

**Counterfactual questioning**: "What if this key assumption were wrong?" "What if this evidence did not exist?" "What if the opposite were true?" Counterfactual reasoning identifies which elements of the argument are load-bearing (their removal would collapse the conclusion) versus decorative (their removal would leave the conclusion intact).

## Evidence Evaluation Criteria

Not all evidence is equal. Systematic evaluation criteria help assess the weight each piece of evidence should carry.

**Relevance**: Does the evidence actually bear on the question at hand? Evidence may be interesting but irrelevant to the specific claim being evaluated. In legal terms, relevant evidence makes a fact "more or less probable than it would be without the evidence."

**Reliability**: How trustworthy is the evidence? Factors include: the source's track record, potential biases, methodology used to generate the evidence, corroboration by independent sources, internal consistency, and consistency with established knowledge. Evidence from a systematic review is generally more reliable than a single study, which is more reliable than an anecdote.

**Sufficiency**: Is there enough evidence to support the conclusion? A single piece of evidence, no matter how strong, may not be sufficient if the claim is extraordinary. Conversely, multiple pieces of weak evidence may collectively provide sufficient support. The evidence should be proportionate to the claim — extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence (Sagan's standard).

**Diagnosticity**: How well does the evidence discriminate between competing hypotheses? Diagnostic evidence is much more likely under one hypothesis than another. Non-diagnostic evidence is equally likely under multiple hypotheses. In investigations, focus on seeking and evaluating diagnostic evidence — it moves the analysis forward, while non-diagnostic evidence, no matter how abundant, does not.

**Provenance**: Where did the evidence come from, and how was it handled? Chain of custody matters for legal proceedings but is also important for analytical integrity. Evidence that has been processed, summarized, or transmitted through multiple intermediaries may have been altered, filtered, or decontextualized.

## Inference Strength Assessment

**Deductive inference**: If the premises are true and the argument is valid, the conclusion is necessarily true. Deduction provides certainty but requires strict logical form and true premises. In practice, most research arguments are not purely deductive.

**Inductive inference**: The premises provide evidence that makes the conclusion probable but not certain. The strength of induction depends on the quantity, quality, and representativeness of the evidence. Inductive arguments are inherently uncertain — the conclusion could be false even if all premises are true (the problem of induction).

**Abductive inference (inference to the best explanation)**: Among competing explanations, prefer the one that best accounts for the available evidence. Evaluation criteria: explanatory scope (how much evidence does it explain?), explanatory power (how well does it explain the evidence?), plausibility (is it consistent with background knowledge?), simplicity (does it avoid unnecessary complexity?), and fertility (does it generate testable predictions?).

**Analogical inference**: Drawing conclusions about one case based on similarity to another case. Strength depends on: the number and relevance of similarities, the absence of critical dissimilarities, and the diversity of cases supporting the analogy. Analogies are useful for generating hypotheses but weak as standalone evidence.

**Inference chains**: Multi-step reasoning where the conclusion of one inference becomes a premise of the next. The overall confidence in the chain is limited by the weakest link — if any step is uncertain, the final conclusion inherits that uncertainty (and compounds it). Long inference chains should be treated with considerable caution. Identify the weakest link and focus verification efforts there.
