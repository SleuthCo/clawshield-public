## The CRAAP Test Framework

The CRAAP test (Currency, Relevance, Authority, Accuracy, Purpose) provides a systematic framework for evaluating information sources. Originally developed for academic library instruction, it applies broadly to any information evaluation context.

**Currency**: When was the information published or last updated? Is the information current enough for the topic? Some topics require very recent information (technology, current events, medical treatments), while others are less time-sensitive (historical analysis, foundational theory). Check: publication date, revision dates, last updated timestamps, and whether links still function. Undated content is a warning sign.

**Relevance**: Does the information address your specific question? Is the level of detail appropriate (not too general, not too narrow)? Is the intended audience compatible with your needs (academic vs. popular, expert vs. general)? Have you consulted a sufficient range of sources before selecting this one? Relevance is context-dependent — a source may be excellent for one question but irrelevant for another.

**Authority**: Who is the author, publisher, or sponsoring organization? What are their qualifications and credentials in this subject area? Is there contact information? Is the author affiliated with a reputable institution? For organizational sources, what is the organization's mission and track record? Check for: academic degrees and positions, professional experience, publication history, peer recognition, and institutional affiliation. Anonymous or pseudonymous sources require additional corroboration.

**Accuracy**: Is the information supported by evidence? Can claims be verified through other sources? Has the information been peer-reviewed or edited? Are citations provided for factual claims? Is the methodology described and sound? Are there factual errors, logical fallacies, or internal contradictions? Does the information align with what is known from other reliable sources?

**Purpose**: Why does this information exist? To inform, persuade, sell, entertain, or deceive? Is the purpose clearly stated? Are there potential conflicts of interest? Is the content objective, or does it present a particular viewpoint? Bias does not automatically disqualify a source — most sources have some perspective — but undisclosed bias undermines trustworthiness. Follow the money: who funds the source, and does funding influence content?

## Lateral Reading Technique

Lateral reading is the strategy expert fact-checkers use to evaluate sources. Rather than spending time analyzing the source itself (vertical reading), fact-checkers quickly leave the page to check what other sources say about the author, publisher, and claims.

**The expert approach**: Professional fact-checkers spend very little time on the original source page. Instead, they open multiple tabs and search for: who is behind the information, what other sources say about the claims, and whether the source has a track record of accuracy or bias. This approach is significantly more effective than evaluating the source's appearance, polish, or self-description.

**Steps in lateral reading**: (1) Stop — do not read the source deeply until you have checked it laterally. (2) Investigate the source — search the author's name, the organization, and the publication. Look for: Wikipedia articles, news coverage, expert commentary, and fact-check results. (3) Find better coverage — search for the specific claim in authoritative sources. If the claim is real, credible sources will have reported it. (4) Trace claims to their origin — follow citations and references back to the primary source.

**Wikipedia as a starting point**: Fact-checkers frequently start with Wikipedia — not to cite it, but to quickly understand what an unfamiliar source or topic is about, identify potential biases, and find links to primary sources. Wikipedia's editorial process, though imperfect, provides a useful initial orientation that can be verified through primary sources.

**Red flags identified through lateral reading**: The source has no independent coverage. The organization was recently created. The author's credentials cannot be verified. Other experts in the field criticize the source. The source has been flagged by fact-checking organizations. The claims appear only in ideologically aligned outlets rather than mainstream or multi-perspective sources.

## Provenance Analysis

Provenance analysis traces the origin and transmission history of information, documents, images, and data.

**Document provenance**: Who created the document? When and where was it created? How has it been transmitted? Has it been altered? Metadata analysis (author, creation date, modification history, software used) provides initial answers. Formatting, language, and stylistic analysis can indicate origin. For leaked or anonymous documents, provenance is critical for assessing authenticity.

**Image provenance**: Reverse image search (Google Images, TinEye, Yandex) identifies the earliest known appearance of an image and tracks its spread. EXIF metadata may contain: camera model, date/time, GPS coordinates, and software used (indicating editing). Image provenance is essential for verifying that images actually depict what they claim to — images are frequently taken out of context or repurposed to support false narratives.

**Information chain analysis**: Trace the information back through each intermediary to the primary source. Each step in the chain introduces potential for: omission, distortion, addition, decontextualization, and fabrication. The longer the chain, the less reliable the final version. Circular reporting — where the same original source is cited through multiple channels, creating the illusion of independent corroboration — is a critical threat.

**Authentication indicators**: For documents: consistent formatting, appropriate terminology for the claimed origin, correct procedural and organizational details, metadata consistent with claimed provenance. For digital media: consistent lighting, shadows, reflections, and perspectives in images and video. For testimony: internal consistency, consistency with known facts, appropriate level of detail (fabricated accounts tend to be either too vague or too detailed).

## Deepfake and Synthetic Media Detection

Synthetic media generated by AI — including deepfake video, audio cloning, AI-generated text, and synthetic images — poses an increasing challenge to source evaluation.

**Deepfake video indicators**: Unnatural blinking patterns or eye movement. Inconsistencies at the boundary between the face and surrounding areas (hairline, ears, neck). Unusual skin texture or coloring. Asymmetric facial features that should be symmetric. Temporal inconsistencies (flickering, morphing between frames). Inconsistent lighting direction on the face vs. the environment. Audio-visual synchronization problems. These indicators are becoming less reliable as technology improves.

**Audio deepfake indicators**: Unnatural prosody (rhythm, stress, intonation). Breathing patterns that do not match speech rhythm. Background noise inconsistencies. Unusual artifacts in spectral analysis. Lack of natural speech dysfluencies (real speech contains hesitations, corrections, and fillers). Audio deepfakes are increasingly convincing, making verification through content and context more important than technical detection alone.

**AI-generated text indicators**: Unnaturally consistent style across a long document. Lack of personal anecdotes or idiosyncratic knowledge. Factual errors presented with high confidence. Hedging patterns typical of language models ("It's important to note that..."). Lack of genuine opinions or emotional nuance. Statistical analysis of writing style can sometimes distinguish human from AI text, though this is an arms race.

**AI-generated image indicators**: Inconsistencies in fine details — hands, fingers, teeth, jewelry, text, and symmetrical objects are challenging for current models. Background inconsistencies and impossible geometry. Inconsistent lighting and shadows. Texture anomalies at high zoom. Metadata lacking camera information or showing AI tool signatures.

**Verification strategies for synthetic media**: Do not rely solely on technical detection — the arms race favors generators. Instead: verify the claim through independent sources, trace the media to its original publication, check whether the depicted events are confirmed by other evidence, contact the purported speaker or subject, and apply normal source evaluation (CRAAP, lateral reading). Consider whether anyone has a motive to create synthetic media in this context.

## Fact-Checking Methodologies

Professional fact-checking organizations follow systematic processes that researchers can adapt.

**Claim identification**: Identify the specific, verifiable claims within a statement. Distinguish claims of fact (verifiable) from claims of opinion (subjective) and claims of prediction (uncertain). Break complex statements into individual checkable claims.

**Source hierarchy for verification**: Primary sources (original data, firsthand accounts, official documents) are strongest. Secondary sources (journalistic reports, academic analyses based on primary data) are valuable but filtered. Tertiary sources (encyclopedias, textbooks, reviews) provide overview but lack specificity. Use the highest-quality sources available for each claim.

**Official records and data**: Government statistics, court filings, regulatory databases, and official reports are generally reliable for the specific data they record, though they may be incomplete, delayed, or subject to political influence. Cross-reference official data across jurisdictions and agencies where possible.

**Expert consultation**: Contact subject matter experts for context, nuance, and evaluation of technical claims. Identify experts through: academic publications, professional organizations, institutional affiliations, and other experts' recommendations. Seek multiple experts to avoid reliance on a single perspective.

**Transparency in fact-checking**: Document the complete methodology: what was checked, how it was checked, what sources were consulted, and how the rating was determined. Provide links to all sources so readers can verify independently. Acknowledge when evidence is ambiguous or when the rating depends on interpretation.

## Media Literacy Frameworks

Media literacy encompasses the skills needed to critically analyze, evaluate, and create media content.

**News Literacy Project framework**: Distinguish between: news (verified, accountable, independent reporting), opinion (clearly labeled subjective analysis), entertainment (not intended as factual), advertising (paid content promoting products or viewpoints), propaganda (systematically biased content serving a political agenda), and raw information (unverified, uncontextualized content). Many problems arise from consuming one type while believing it is another.

**SIFT method** (Mike Caulfield): Stop (pause before sharing or acting on information), Investigate the source (use lateral reading to check who is behind the content), Find better coverage (search for the claim in authoritative sources), Trace claims to their original context (follow citations and references upstream). SIFT is designed for quick, efficient evaluation in digital environments.

**Information disorder taxonomy** (Wardle and Derakhshan): Misinformation (false content shared without harmful intent — honest mistakes, satire taken seriously). Disinformation (false content created and shared deliberately to cause harm — propaganda, fabricated news, manipulated media). Malinformation (genuine content shared to cause harm — leaks, harassment, hate speech). Each type requires different responses.

**Propaganda techniques**: Name-calling, glittering generalities, transfer (associating with respected symbols), testimonial (celebrity endorsement), plain folks (appearing ordinary), card stacking (selective presentation of evidence), bandwagon (everyone is doing it), and appeal to fear. Modern propaganda also exploits: algorithmic amplification, information flooding (drowning signal in noise), and astroturfing (manufactured grassroots support).

## Source Reliability and Information Credibility Ratings

Intelligence communities use standardized scales to separate two dimensions of source evaluation: the reliability of the source and the credibility of the specific information.

**Source reliability scale (NATO/Admiralty system)**:
- A: Completely reliable (no doubt about the source's authenticity, trustworthiness, or competence)
- B: Usually reliable (minor doubt; source has been reliable in the past with very few exceptions)
- C: Fairly reliable (some doubt; source has provided valid information in the past but not consistently)
- D: Not usually reliable (significant doubt; source has been unreliable in the past)
- E: Unreliable (source has a history of providing invalid information)
- F: Reliability cannot be judged (no basis for evaluating the source)

**Information credibility scale**:
- 1: Confirmed (confirmed by independent sources; logical in itself; consistent with other information)
- 2: Probably true (not confirmed; logical in itself; consistent with other information)
- 3: Possibly true (not confirmed; reasonably logical; agrees with some other information)
- 4: Doubtfully true (not confirmed; possible but not logical; no other information on the topic)
- 5: Improbable (not confirmed; not logical; contradicted by other information)
- 6: Truth cannot be judged (no basis for evaluating the information's validity)

**Applying the dual rating**: Always rate source and information separately. An unreliable source (E) may occasionally provide confirmed information (1), and a reliable source (A) may occasionally provide doubtful information (4). The combination (e.g., B2, C3) communicates both dimensions efficiently. Re-evaluate ratings as new information emerges.

## Verification in Digital Environments

The digital information environment presents unique verification challenges including the speed of information spread, the ease of fabrication, and the volume of content.

**URL analysis**: Check the actual domain — not the displayed link text. Look for: impersonation domains (slight misspellings of legitimate sites), misleading subdomains, and unusual top-level domains. Check WHOIS registration for domain age, owner, and registrar. Recently registered domains are higher risk.

**Social media verification**: Check account creation date, posting history, follower patterns, and engagement metrics. Bot indicators: high posting frequency, repetitive content, unusual activity times, and disproportionate follower-to-engagement ratios. Coordinated inauthentic behavior shows: identical or near-identical content across multiple accounts, synchronized timing, and artificial amplification patterns.

**Geolocation verification**: Verify claimed locations using: satellite imagery (Google Earth, Sentinel Hub), street-level imagery (Google Street View, Mapillary), shadow analysis (SunCalc — sun position at the claimed time and location), visible landmarks, signage language, vegetation, architecture, vehicle types, and weather verification (check historical weather records for the claimed location and time).

**Temporal verification**: Verify when events occurred using: metadata timestamps (check for manipulation), archived web content (Wayback Machine — was the content available before the claimed date?), satellite imagery time series, and corroborating reports with independent timestamps. Be alert to recycled content — old images and videos republished as if they depict current events.
